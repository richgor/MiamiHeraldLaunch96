Published Friday, May 10, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

For U.S., China trade is crucial

By JAMES R. CARROLL
Herald Washington Bureau

WASHINGTON -- The current dispute between President Clinton and congressional critics over whether China should have the same trade benefits as most other American trading partners is a small fight with potentially enormous consequences.

While China is the fifth-largest trading partner with the United States, trade between the two nations is a relatively small part of overall American trade.

But the long-term prospect of business with the world's most populous nation makes the question of how we manage that trade very important.

``Projections are that by 2010, they will be the world's largest economy, exceeding our own,'' a White House official said. ``It would be foolish to ignore that.''

In 1995, U.S.-China trade totaled $57.2 billion -- nearly 80 percent of it imports from China.

That trade represented just 4 percent of the $1.3 trillion in total U.S. trade with other nations, but in the trade world, ``that's a big number,'' said Peter Morici, director of the University of Maryland's Center for International Business Education and Research.

Even more important, he said, the U.S. market represents about a third of China's total exports.

The money China is making selling goods to the United States is helping to pay for its modernization, Morici said.

If its exports to the United States are cut, he said, ``Where would they get the money to buy the planes, the construction machinery, the electric generators and trucks they do buy from us?''

The current dispute centers on low U.S. tariffs granted to China thanks to its status as a Most Favored Nation trading partner. Virtually every country with which America trades has that status.

Clinton is poised to review China's status, and some in Congress think it should be denied to send the world's largest communist regime a message on human rights abuses, copyright piracy, nuclear weapons proliferation and threats against Taiwan and Hong Kong.

Republican presidential candidate and Senate Majority Leader Bob Dole on Thursday threw his weight behind most-favored-nation renewal for China, resisting entreaties from a spectrum of opponents, including some conservatives in his own party.
