Published Tuesday, May 7, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

New book challenges the hopeful entrepreneur

By PATTY SHILLINGTON
Herald Business Writer

Tom Culley, author of Beating the Odds in Small Business, is attempting to do just that.

His small business is his new book, an opus with an attitude.

He abandoned his consulting work to write it. He laid it out on a computer in the bedroom-office of his Coconut Grove apartment. He formed a company, Small Business Matters Inc., to print the book after contracts with two publishers could not survive his exacting style.

Now, he's marketing Beating the Odds with a feverish persistence, following his book's advice to put your new small business first.

In colorful, and sometimes off-color, language (Culley even manages to slip a four-letter word onto the cover), the 412-page text describes the ``realities of the business jungle.'' The book, like its author, is funny and irreverent, opinionated and pushy. He backs up his points with blurbs from newspapers and magazines.

``The book is the result of a whole lot of things that happened to me and made me weird enough to write it,'' says Culley, 57, sitting in the apartment he shares with three Siamese cats. ``This book was written out of conviction. I'm also a businessman. I saw an opportunity.''

Culley aims his message at ``Middle America,'' including laid-off workers disillusioned with the corporate world. And the message to people starting their own business is: ``You have to be utterly cynical.''

``The book is designed to prevent disaster, and if I need to be brutally frank to do it, so be it,'' he says. ``And if that offends some people, so be it.''

Culley concludes in Chapter Four (The Welcoming Committee: Watch Your Back . . . Hide Your Wallet) that established businesses intentionally rip off ``green and gullible'' newcomers to such an extent that it's the ``principal underlying cause of most small business failures'' in their first two years. Culley calls this ``The Cannibal Syndrome.''

He writes of lawyers as ``plagues,'' franchising as ``risky'' and overrated. He warns potential business owners to have the proper capital to stay alive: ``Profitable, well-managed, beautiful little businesses can and do die the dumbest of deaths. They die of thirst.''

Culley's direct manner has attracted fans like Phyllis Borenstein, who with her husband Richard, ``a frustrated accountant,'' opened The Big Tomato, a carry-out cafe in South Dade specializing in gourmet tomato pies and salads. The business is almost 2 years old and ``over the hump finan

cially.''

Borenstein said Culley's Dear Reader letter brought tears to her eyes. She was so moved by his understanding of the small-business plight that she faxed Culley a letter. ``I wish I would have had this book two years ago. I would have been a lot more prepared,'' says Borenstein, a one-time Realtor.

Borenstein says she needed his advice on personnel -- Employees Make or Break Your Business -- before starting her cafe. ``I was so busy worrying about making a product somebody would love, I didn't even think about employees,'' she says. ``Employees are very stressful.''

Culley was born in Brazil to British parents. His father ran a flour mill. At 10, he was shipped off to boarding school in England, returning at 17 to work in a family insurance business.

At 24, he persuaded the Wharton School of Business at the University of Pennsylvania to admit him into the MBA program even though he had never been to college. ``I studied terribly hard, and I graduated first in my class,'' he says. (Wharton doesn't keep class rankings but confirmed he graduated in 1965.) ``It was determination and fear that got me through.''

With a business partner who had capital, Culley returned to Brazil and started developing convenience stores. ``We were a huge success and then we went bankrupt. That taught me much more than anything I learned at Wharton. We were too cocky. We weren't watching the cash flow.''

Culley worked for management consultants McKinsey & Co. in Ohio and Portugal before returning to Brazil to work for, start and run a variety of small companies. He is divorced, with three grown children, and recently settled in Miami.

Mitchell Kaplan, owner of Books & Books in Coral Gables and Miami Beach, has helped Culley promote his book. Culley gave out Books & Books' phone number when he was on a talk radio show that aired in St. Louis. ``We got tons of calls and letters,'' Kaplan says, ``which tells me that people respond to him in some way. He translates some of his own dynamism into the book, and he's a pretty dynamic guy.''

Culley says he and three partners have invested $100,000 into his book venture. He published 5,000 copies. The book sells for $29.95; as a self-publisher, he nets $15 on each sale. His book recently was accepted by Barnes & Noble, the nation's biggest bookstore chain.

Marcella Smith, business director of small presses for Barnes & Noble, says ``it's too soon to say'' if Culley's work will stand out among other business books.

Culley says Beating the Odds will beat the odds if people want the cold truth instead of ``sentimental goo.''

``Small business has become trendy; it's the object of hype. Nobody says: `It's rough, it's hard.' The question is,'' he says, ``do people want to read about reality or do they want to read about dreams?''
