Published Saturday, May 11, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Florist gets left out in the cold

By AUDRA D.S. BURCH
Herald Staff Writer

A florist by any other name is simply not the same.

Just ask Exotic Gardens, a local chain where owners are facing Mother's Day, the biggest floral holiday of the year, without their name in the Greater Miami Yellow Pages.

To make matters worse, a similar-sounding company -- Boston-based Exotic Flowers -- has a large ad and listing in the phone book. That name is drawing unsuspecting Exotic Gardens customers. Ironically, local orders placed with the out-of-town company are routed to Exotic Gardens. However, customers calling the Boston company to place orders for local deliveries may end up paying a wire transfer fee or higher arrangement prices.

The result: A very thorny situation.

``Our business has definitely been impacted by this,'' said Bonnie Jones, owner of the four Exotic Gardens stores. ``One of the big problems is that Exotic Flowers is based in Massachusetts, but has a local number listed in the book. So people think they are calling us. It's certainly confusing.''

Exotic Flowers officials did not return messages left by The Herald.

Alan Krevis, a five-year Exotic Gardens customer, called Exotic Flowers to send an arrangement for Hanukkah.

``First, I asked for the person who I normally deal with,'' he recalled. ``They said he was not in. When they asked my name, they said there was no record of previous orders I had made. I thought that was strange since I had just ordered arrangements for my parents wedding anniversary in November,'' Krevis said. ``Once I realized they were not Exotic Gardens, I didn't bother to place the order.''

Jones and BellSouth officials said an ``oversight'' resulted in Exotic Gardens' name and Miami phone number not being listed in the Yellow Pages, which is distributed each August.

BellSouth spokesman Spero Canton said Exotic Flowers is allowed to use a local number because it notes in the ad that it is based in Boston.

Last April -- before the ad ran -- the local florist filled 11 Exotic Flowers orders. This April, that number jumped to 157, Jones said.

Jones said not much can be done except to send a notice to the 30,000 customers on her mailing list. But that would be costly.

``This is a mess,'' she said. ``All I can do is wait for the next telephone book to come out.''
