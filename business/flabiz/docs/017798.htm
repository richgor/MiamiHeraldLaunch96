  • The company sold its underperforming HMOs in Georgia and Alabama to Health Partners of Alabama for $25 million in a deal expected to close in July. While the HMOs accounted for only 4 percent of first-quarter revenues, they lost $2.5 million during that period, the company said. PCA will continue its ``strategy of being a dominant HMO player in our core markets of Florida, Texas and Puerto Rico,'' Kardatzke said. Proceeds from both transactions will be used to pay down PCA's $160 million debt.

  • Another indicator of HMO strength also improved. PCA's medical loss ratio -- the percentage of premiums spent on medical care -- dipped to 87 percent, down from 90 percent in the fourth quarter of last year. PCA said it is also spending less on administration.

  • PCA may also be getting legislative relief. Last week, the Florida Legislature passed a measure requiring all the state's 1.6 million Medicaid recipients be enrolled in a managed care plan to cut costs. Gov. Lawton Chiles must give final approval. Medicaid pays doctor and hospital bills for Florida's poor and disabled and is jointly funded by the state and federal governments. PCA had been left reeling after the state cut reimbursements to all Medicaid HMOs. The state concurrently slapped Medicaid HMO plans with marketing restrictions to curb enrollment fraud, a move the HMOs said made it more difficult to find new members and fueled enrollment declines.

  • Membership in PCA's health plans rose to 918,000, compared with 646,000 the previous year. PCA also operates commercial, Medicare and workers compensation health plans.
