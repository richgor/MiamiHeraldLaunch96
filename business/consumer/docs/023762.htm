Published Tuesday, May 7, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Seven professions happy to stay regulated

By AUDRA D.S. BURCH
Herald Staff Writer

An ambitious state plan to deregulate seven professions was introduced, amended, debated, then died in the Florida Legislature.

The net effect: Business as usual for talent agents, yacht and ship brokers, auctioneers, interior designers, landscape architects, time-share solicitors and health-care service (temporary employment) pools.

``We knew going in it was going to be a tough sale,'' said Ed Towey, spokesman for the Florida Department of Business and Professional Regulation. ``One by one, the professions were picked out of the bill. It was finally pronounced dead on Saturday. Everything stays the same.''

That means the department will continue to license the business trades and accept consumer complaints, and various professional boards will discipline violators.

The deregulation proposal capped an eight-month study to streamline the department, targeting business trades that receive few complaints.

State officials said the monitoring of 36 professions was costly and time-consuming, and it was not the state's responsibility to improve specific industries. The seven professions represented only 6,500 of the 1.1 million licensees statewide.

But professionals, who mounted formidable campaigns against the proposal, said the two-year licensing fees -- ranging from $50 to $500 -- fund the cost of regulations. And those rules are needed to maintain standards and build a credible industry. It's also good for business; consumers often equate a license or regulations with quality service.

``We are ecstatic about the regulations staying in place. Ten years ago, our rules were sunsetted and it was a zoo, with people selling boats out of the back of their trunks,'' said Peter Grimm, legislative representative and former president of the Florida Yacht Brokers Association. ``If we had been deregulated, it would have gone back to Dodge City.''
