Published Friday, May 3, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

American Express woos banks

Move to lure card issuers riles Visa

Herald Wire Services

In an effort to expand its credit and charge-card operations, American Express Co. announced Thursday that it would actively court banks to issue its cards.

The announcement -- made by American Express chairman Harvey Golub at a credit card conference in Atlanta -- took public a campaign the company has been quietly waging for about a year. It also raised interest in the banking community and riled Visa USA, an Amex competitor.

``They got religion late and realized it was a product they needed to offer (through banks) in order to survive,'' said Susan Murdy, a spokeswoman for Visa. ``They're trying to catch up, but they don't have the distribution network'' that Visa does, she said.

If banks were to issue American Express charge cards and Optima credit cards, they would be competing with their own Visa and Mastercards. Moreover, the move would break a Visa rule that bans its member banks from offering American Express cards and Dean Witter, Discover & Co.'s Dean Witter Discover cards. Mastercard does not have a comparable rule.

``Maybe not every bank will want to work with us,'' said American Express vice chairman Kenneth Chenault. ``But we have substantially increased our overall business in the past year. And we have a lot to offer the banks. We already have an incredibly successful travelers check business with the banks.''

The idea received mixed reviews from some large banks.

Charles Walsh, head of Chase Manhattan Corp.'s Cardmembers Service Group, said the nation's No. 1 bank was not interested. He said Chase's Visa and MasterCard programs, which together have $24 billion in outstanding billings, ``support all of the needs of our customers.''

But John B. McCoy, chairman and chief executive of Banc One, said his Columbus, Ohio-based company has tested an American Express preapproved credit line for small businesses and sold its credit-processing systems to American Express. ``I have a tendency to listen when they speak,'' McCoy said.

While Amex's play is controversial, it could help solve its problems. A spate of bank-issued cards would not only increase American Express' revenues, it would reverse a slide in market share. Since 1985, American Express' share of the charge and credit card market, measured in point-of-sale retail sales, has fallen from 28 percent to 18.4 percent.
