Published Sunday, May 5, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Sudanese plane crashes in sandstorm, kills 53

KHARTOUM, Sudan -- (AP) -- A Sudanese passenger plane, attempting an emergency landing during a sandstorm, crashed in a field, killing all 53 people aboard, officials said Saturday.

The private Federal Airlines jet was en route to Khartoum when it crashed at 10 p.m. Friday. Among the dead were 26 students at Bahr el-Ghazal University in southern Sudan.

Sand had covered the runways of Khartoum airport, so the pilot of the Russian-made Antonov 24 tried to land in an area cleared for construction of a new airport outside the capital, officials said.

The pilot crashed because of poor visibility, Brig. el-Tayeb Ibrahim Mohamed Kheir, Sudan's information minister, told Egypt's Middle East News Agency.

The crash killed 48 passengers and five crew members, all of them Sudanese, Kheir said.

The jet struck a newly built empty house near the cleared field, the officials said. No deaths or injuries were reported on the ground.

Among the dead was Moheideen Abdullah al-Faqeeh, a retired general who was the manager of Federal Airlines.

The flight originated at Wau, the capital of Bahr el-Ghazal state about 600 miles southwest of Khartoum.

The United States and other Western countries accuse Sudan's Muslim fundamentalist government of supporting Islamic militants, and Sudan is on the U.S. State Department's list of countries that sponsor terrorism.

On Feb. 26, a C-130 transport plane belonging to the Sudanese military crashed at Jebel Aulia, 24 miles south of the capital. All 91 people aboard were killed.

Military officials blamed that crash on mechanical problems.
