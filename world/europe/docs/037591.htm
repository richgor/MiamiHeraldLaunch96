Published Sunday, April 21, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Italy's old politics are dead; what's next?

Today's vote may end paralysis

ROME -- (AP) -- When Italians vote today in the third national election in four years, the biggest victory would be an end to the political paralysis that has dashed hopes for radical change.

The last year has been a sobering time after the seemingly earth-shaking political events of the early 1990s, when a far-reaching corruption scandal brought down Italy's main parties and led to some electoral reforms.

Those heady days vaporized when politicians became mired in the same factionalism and government instability that has existed since World War II.

The hope is that finally a clear political majority will emerge and form a strong government able to act decisively.

Italians may be disappointed.

The two main political blocs were ranked dead even in polls before the March 31 deadline on publishing opinion surveys.

If neither side finishes strongly enough to form a government, politicians say they will try to rewrite election laws once again to ensure a clear outcome the next time.

After the corruption scandal, Parliament changed the electoral system to pick all senators and three-fourths of the Chamber of Deputies by majority vote in single-member districts. The other deputies are allotted to parties based on their proportion of the total vote -- the way all seats were doled out in the previous system -- and that allowed numerous small parties to keep footholds in Parliament.

But enlarging on reforms may not be easy. President Oscar Luigi Scalfaro called this election three years early after both sides failed to agree on reforms in February.

The election will mark the fortunes of one man in particular: Silvio Berlusconi, the media magnate who founded his own party and won the last election in 1994 against all expectations. He served as premier for eight months before a coalition ally withdrew support, forcing him to resign.

Berlusconi, on trial for corruption charges that he calls a political vendetta, has hinted he will walk away from politics if he loses this time.

He heads a center-right coalition made up of his Forza Italy (Let's Go Italy) party; the National Alliance, descended from Italy's neo-fascist movement; and a handful of conservative-leaning former Christian Democrats.

The former Communists dominate the rival center-left coalition, which includes another slice of the ex-Christian Democratic party and the hard-line Communist Refoundation.

The center-left proposes an economist and former state industrialist, Romano Prodi, for the premiership.

Swing votes could come from defectors of the Northern League, a regional-based populist party that soared in the early 1990s but has declined recently.

Italy has been without an elected government since Berlusconi's resignation in December 1994. Berlusconi's ex-treasury minister, Lamberto Dini, was installed as the head of a nonaligned ``technocrat'' government.

Dini, now acting premier, is running for the Chamber of Deputies with the center-left.
