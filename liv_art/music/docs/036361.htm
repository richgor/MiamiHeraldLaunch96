Published Friday, May 3, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Secada, Green still on concert docket

By HOWARD COHEN
Herald Staff Writer

SunFest -- the mega festival of art, music and water -- isn't over yet. Yes, Joan Osborne, The Band and Lisa Loeb did their thing earlier this week, but music fans still have a weekend full of acts to tide them over until, well, next year. Here's a look:

AL GREEN

  • 7:30 tonight on the NationsBank Stage.

    Reverend Al is back in the secular business, and would ya believe, today's kids are hearing the minister's word? His Greatest Hits CD has been riding Billboard's R&B album chart for 38 weeks, thanks in no small part to Quentin Tarantino's use of Green's '70s soul classic Let's Stay Together in the trendy Pulp Fiction flick.
    E.J. WATERS

  • 9 p.m. Saturday on the Fidelity Federal Stage.

    OK, comparing anyone to the force of nature that was Janis Joplin is plain unfair, but Miami-raised, New Jersey-born Ellen (E.J.) Waters bears a slight vocal resemblance when she belts out the pop/rock material on her self-titled debut CD. It's a solid collection, surpassing the work she did as a vocalist for forgotten dance band Will to Power (I'm Not in Love).
    JON SECADA

  • 8:15 p.m. Sunday on the NationsBank Stage.

    Who you might not expect to see cheering Secada's SunFest set: Maybe Latin balladeer Julio Iglesias and Mexican rock group Maná. Unless they are good sports, that is. Secada's Spanish album, Amor, triumphed over the other two popular acts at this year's Grammy Awards in the Latin Pop category.
    BRUCE HORNSBY

  • 9 p.m. Saturday on the NationsBank Stage.

    Hornsby, a former University of Miami music school student, and former Top 40 resident (The Way It Is), has turned his attention to less commercially lucrative pop/jazz on his last two CDs. The pianist (you might remember his work on Don Henley's The End of the Innocence) is a fun concert act, though. He's known to bring fans up on stage to dance. So, if you're going, dress to impress.
    SPECIAL EFX

  • 5 p.m. Saturday on the Florida Crystals Stage.

    Contemporary jazz percussionist George Jinda introduces new members of his ensemble at SunFest. The Budapest-born musician uses Udu drums (a series of clay pots), tuned wood blocks, chimes and bells to create a sound heard on the latest Special EFX CD, Body Language.
