Published Tuesday, May 7, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Citizens should stop abusing parking spots for the handicapped

A few weeks ago, I wrote about people parking illegally in handicap spots.

We touched a chord. Many who wrote were from Broward County, which was one of the earliest places in the country to begin a ``Citizens Patrolling Unit.''

As police officer Robert Labelle of Broward wrote, ``I see on a daily basis the abuses of the handicap law.'' Particularly at large shopping malls.

Many of you are astounded by the number of young people who drive up in their convertibles, kids in tow, placard in place and hop out to merrily do errands.

As previously stated, such parking is not a privilege, but an issue of safety. If I could walk, I would gladly park elsewhere. But, because I am so low to the ground in my wheelchair, I am often below a driver's range of vision.

The point is this: If it were safe for persons with disabilities to park elsewhere, we would. Many of you wrote about the doctors who easily dole out prescriptions for handicap placards. Yes, doctors are a little free in doing so, fearing they might lose their patient -- or friend -- if they say no. Unfortunately, greed has a louder voice than ethics in this case.

Wouldn't it be interesting if one of the patients was a spy for 20/20? What a great show it would be -- the doctors being filmed while freely giving away prescriptions for handicap parking placards.

Many of you also said there should be stricter enforcement. By whom? Keep in mind that Dade County has more doctors than police. And the police are overwhelmed.

We should follow a program run by the Pembroke Pines Police Department. This involves volunteers who work as a ``handicapped parking specialists,'' much like the county's program. One specialist who wrote, Alan Rose, gives away an average two tickets each time he is on patrol.

Obviously, these volunteers relieve the police on duty, plus channel any anger in a good way. Volunteers are needed, everywhere and all the time.

Like the ``Take Back Our Streets'' movement sweeping the country, it's time we do something about this pervasive malady that is equally sweeping the country.

In other words, we need to stop complaining and pointing fingers, and start doing.
