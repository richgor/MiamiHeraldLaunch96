Published Tuesday, May 7, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Help for caregivers moving loved ones to long-term facilities

P
lacing a spouse, parent or other family member in a long-term care facility often is as emotionally difficult on the caregiver as it is on the person who is ill.

But there comes a time when the caregiver can no longer control the behavior of the loved one suffering from a chronic, degenerative disease.

Nevertheless, guilt, ambivalence and grief often accompany the decision to place a loved one in a nursing home.

To help with that change, support groups are important, suggests the Family Caregiver Alliance. Such groups can provide information and encouragement concerning:

  • Family and patient rights and how to advocate for them.

  • Methods to ensure ongoing quality care.

  • Interacting with members of the staff at the facility.

  • How to begin to develop a way of life beyond caregiving.

    That last is important because many caregivers are coping with problems often brought on by continuing emotional strain.

    The stress comes from the difficulty in caring for those with illnesses such as Alzheimer's, 65 percent of the time while the caregiver is holding down a job. More than half of the people who were institutionalized suffered from Alzheimer's.

    Slightly more of the patients were women. Seventy-five percent of them were 75 or older and 70 percent of them had incomes of less than $16,000 a year.

    After a loved one was placed in the facility, the former caregiver visited the sick person, on average, three times a week.
