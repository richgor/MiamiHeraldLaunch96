** 1/2 This period piece, set mostly in post-World War I England, is a doleful study of unrequited love, built around painter Dora Carrington's fawning worship of author Lytton Strachey (Jonathan Pryce). She (Emma Thompson) was a woman superhumanly unselfish -- sharing not only her country cottages with the homosexual Strachey but also the charms of her husband (Steven Waddington). It's worth seeing just to hear what stinging observation the Eminent Victorians author will unleash next. -- Jackie Potts (R; vulgar language, sexual situations, brief nudity.) (1995, Gramercy/PolyGram, $96.99, 122 minutes; available on video disc.)


DEAD PRESIDENTS
*** Larenz Tate, Keith David and Chris Tucker star. A young black man enlists in the Marines in 1969, serves in Vietnam and returns home to the Bronx in 1972 -- a time of recession and revolution. This ambitious film palpably depicts the evolution of a happy-go-lucky teenager into hardened warrior into conflicted vet and packs enough story for two epics. -- Knight-Ridder News Service (R; extreme violence, war and gore, drugs, discreet sex, candor, profanity.) (1995, Hollywood, 120 minutes, available on video disc.)

IT TAKES TWO
** 1/2 TV's chirpy Olsen twins revive Hayley Mills' dual roles in this Parent Trap-inspired romp, which, despite its relentless cheer, is hard to resist. Mary-Kate is a gum-chewing, tomboy orphan and Ashley is the oh-so-polite daughter of a dapper, cellular-phone magnate (Steve Guttenberg). The two collide, literally, at a summer camp and conspire to pull the ol' switcheroo, hoping to match the orphanage's winsome case worker (Kirstie Alley) with the heiress' hunky dad (Guttenberg) and ditch his shrewish fiance (Friends' Jane Sibbet) in the bargain. -- Jackie Potts (PG; no offensive material.) (1995,Warner, $22.98, 101 minutes; available on video disc.)

THE SHOW
** Director Brian Robbins (Head of the Class) eavesdrops into the private lives of top hip-hop acts for a look at a musical world that is part entertainment, part way out of the 'hood and part way of life. The sociological analysis is sometimes successful; the concert footage stumbles. -- Howard Cohen (R; adult themes, vulgar language, drug use.) (1996, Columbia Tristar, 91 minutes; available on video disc.)

RECENT MOVIES

Dead Cold (R; 1996, LIVE, $94.98, 91 minutes). Lysette Anthony, Chris Mulkey. A frazzled Hollywood writer takes his wife to a mountain cabin for a rest, only to discover his wife is not the woman he thought she was.

A Pig's Tale (PG; 1994, PolyGram, $89.99, 94 minutes). Joe Flaherty, Graham Sack. No, not another talking pig movie. This is your basic summer camp misadventures of a typical kid who ends up assigned to a cabin of misfits known as The Pig Hut.

VINTAGE FILMS

Come September (1961, MCA/Universal, $14.98, 114 minutes). Rock Hudson, Gina Lollobrigida. Rock is an American businessman whose annual holiday on the Italian Riviera is turned upside down.

Lover Come Back (1961, MCA/Universal, $14.98, 107 minutes; available on video disc). The best and funniest of the Rock Hudson-Doris Day romantic comedies, set in the world of Madison Avenue advertising. Hudson is a lovable stud, trying to trick Doris out of her knickers.

Strange Bedfellows (1965, MCA/Universal, $14.98, 114 minutes). Rock Hudson, Gina Lollobrigida. Fair bedroom farce about an American oil exec who can't take his fiery Italian wife's dabbling in radical politics.

MADE FOR TELEVISION

The Right to Remain Silent (R; 1996, Republic, 96 minutes). Lea Thompson, Robert Loggia. Made-for-Showtime drama of a rookie booking officer who gets an earful of stories about abuse, loneliness and injustice on her first night on the job.
