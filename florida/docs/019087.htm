Published Thursday, May 9, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Governor may veto package of tax breaks

By BILL BERGSTROM
Associated Press

TALLAHASSEE -- Hotels, oil drillers, Internet services and a fish museum that tacked tax breaks onto a huge bill as the Legislature closed better not count their money yet, Gov. Lawton Chiles said Wednesday.

In the closing chaos, lawmakers turned a $1.5 million business-promoting tax break package approved by Senate committees into a ``tax train'' with enough loopholes hitched on to bring exemptions and credits to $45 million.

``I would say it may be a train going to nowhere,'' Chiles said.

The governor pointed out that lawmakers slashed social services for the needy by $400 million in the $39.8 billion state budget they finally passed.

Chiles questioned showering businesses with tax breaks ``at a time when we don't know if we can run the Department of Health and Rehabilitative Services because of cuts we've made.''

Lt. Gov. Buddy MacKay said an analysis received by his office showed many of the tax train exemptions differed from those granted in a major economic development package passed at the end of the session.

Those breaks -- such as a sales tax exemption on electricity used in manufacturing -- were favored by the administration based on what they would do for economic development, he said.

But the exemptions hitched to the tax train didn't all meet that standard, and that may lead to a veto, said MacKay, reached in Washington.

``The more you overload that train, the greater the risk of derailment,'' he said. ``This one is pretty heavily loaded.''

The package does include things the administration likes, such as a year's delay in taxing Internet services to businesses while a study panel examines the overall telecommunications tax picture.

It includes benefits for ports and for enterprise zones that give companies incentives for creating jobs in economically depressed areas.

``Those are some of the things we'll have to weigh,'' Chiles said.

If the Internet tax study dies with the tax train, Revenue director Larry Fuchs will ask Chiles to create a study panel by executive order, spokesman Chuck Springston said.

But it hasn't been decided whether the tax would be collected while the study is being done.

Other items, such as a five-year oil and gas severance tax exemption designed to encourage petroleum exploration, may not be doomed even if the bill fails.

``Hopefully not,'' said Brian Bibeau, lobbyist for the Florida Independent Petroleum Producers. ``It's on another bill as well.''

Once Chiles considers how such incentives could stimulate the economy, Bibeau said, ``We feel hopefully he's going to sign it.''

He added that many of the ``train'' provisions were from other bills that did win committee approval, or were passed by the House or Senate, and just ran out of time for final passage.

That was the case with a sales tax exemption for complementary meals included in hotel bills, said Jim Tillman of the Florida Hotel and Motel Association.

Tillman said the House passed a bill granting the break for the meals -- such as the continental breakfast or kids-eat-free meals included in some room packages. A Senate bill won committee approval.

But time ran out to finish passage of those bills, so the exemption was hitched to the tax train.

Like the beneficiaries of more than 40 other breaks in the bill, said Tillman, ``We of course are keeping our fingers crossed that the governor won't veto it, but we are just one small item in there.''
