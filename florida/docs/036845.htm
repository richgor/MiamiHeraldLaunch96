Published Thursday, May 9, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Duval board agrees to desegregation plan

Associated Press

JACKSONVILLE -- A new desegregation agreement between the Duval County School Board and the NAACP calls for construction of 14 schools.

The plan mandates black enrollment of 20 percent to 55 percent at targeted schools.

By the end of 1996-97 school year, 106 of 148 schools must meet desegregation goals, up from the current 93.

By voting 5-1 to approve the plan Tuesday night, the School Board took a major step toward ending its decades-long dispute with the NAACP on how best to desegregate Jacksonville schools. No busing is required.

``I'll feel total relief once the NAACP takes the vote,'' said School Board Chairwoman Cheryl Donelan.

The pact worked out during the weekend after three months of court-ordered mediation must still win approval from local and national officials of the National Association for the Advancement of Colored People.

The vote to approve the new schools kicks off a seven-year, $318 million building plan.

Some board members and parents have long urged new construction to relieve crowding in fast-growing areas, but the proposals have been held up by the dispute.

Both sides were ordered into mediation in January for failing to meet goals set in 1990.

The NAACP plays a key role because it is the plaintiff in a 35-year-old lawsuit against the board.

The local chapter's executive committee is expected to vote on the proposal later this week. The general membership, branch administrator and national legal counsel also must agree.

The proposal is slightly different from a version adopted by the School Board last month but never approved by the NAACP.

Leaders of the civil rights group feared the earlier version would not do enough to ensure the board would meet integration goals.

Both proposals called for building the new schools over seven years and pumping $10.8 million into magnet schools and steadily increasing the number of schools that would meet desegregation goals.

Six schools would be built in the first two years of the plan, including one in a predominantly black neighborhood. Three of the 14 new schools are in blacks areas.
