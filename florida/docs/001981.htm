Published Thursday, May 9, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Date-rape pill may be reclassified

By JACKIE HALLIFAX
Associated Press

TALLAHASSEE -- A sedative known as the date-rape pill can be classified with dangerous drugs like heroin, though the Legislature does not agree, Attorney General Bob Butterworth said Wednesday.

Butterworth has the power to pass a rule moving Rohypnol from a category of drugs with a low potential for abuse -- like Valium -- into the toughest category.

The Legislature has classified Rohypnol, nicknamed roofies, as a drug with some medical use. But Butterworth wants to classify the illegal drug as one with no medical value.

A Broward County prosecutor has asked Butterworth to crack down on the illegal drug.

Broward and the rest of South Florida is ``experiencing an epidemic of abuse of the drug,'' according to State Attorney Michael Satz.

Between October 1994 and last September, federal drug agents in Miami seized about 42,000 roofie tablets. Between last October and February, more than 136,000 tablets were seized.

``Our office has six pending sexual battery cases, which are directly linked to `roofies,' '' Satz wrote Butterworth on Tuesday. ``A defendant who was recently convicted and sentenced bragged that he had raped many women after slipping them a `roofie.' ''

Roofies have been used in several sexual assaults in the South Florida town of Cooper City, including one in which three teenagers were charged in March with repeatedly raping a girl after crashing a Sweet 16 party and spiking her drink with the sedative.

Used in some 60 counties to alleviate insomnia, the drug is not approved for use in the United States. At least 10 times stronger than Valium, the sedative can impair a person's ability to move, think and react in addition to causing memory loss.

Early Saturday, in the final hours of the two-month legislative session, lawmakers passed a bill keeping roofies classified with drugs that have an accepted medical use and whose abuse may lead to limited physical or psychological dependence.

However, lawmakers increased the penalties to equal those reserved for drugs with no accepted medical use and a high potential for abuse. Using the drug is now a first- or second-degree felony, which carries stiff prison terms.

Gov. Lawton Chiles has not acted on the legislation.

But Butterworth warned Wednesday that splitting the classification and the penalty is unprecedented, and prosecutors didn't think the tougher penalties could be applied.

Butterworth has filed a notice of intent to adopt an administrative rule to strengthen Rohypnol's classification. His office also has scheduled a public hearing for June 17 in Fort Lauderdale.

On the federal level, the U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration is taking steps to reclassify Rohypnol as a drug with no accepted medical use in the United States.
