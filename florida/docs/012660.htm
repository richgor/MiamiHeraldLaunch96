Published Saturday, May 11, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

More of judge's decisions criticized

Associated Press

TAMPA -- A judge facing disciplinary action for jailing a woman who cried in court faces criticism for giving custody of three children to a father accused of abusing them.

The judge said he was troubled by the mother's pregnancy by another man and by her work at night.

The new baby of Michelle Dudek would interfere with raising her other three children, who need ``a man's presence,'' Hillsborough Circuit Judge J.T. Taylor wrote in a ruling giving custody to her ex-husband, Gary Lee Dudek.

The mother has started a second family, which will ``interfere considerably'' with the present family unit and cause two allegiances as far as the children are concerned, Taylor said.

The judge also penalized the mother for having a job that required her to work nights.

Besides losing her children, Michelle Dudek was ordered to pay $450 a month child support.

Authorities in California previously had determined the father physically abused the children and their mother. They obtained a court order to prevent the father from having unsupervised contact with the children.

But Taylor disagreed and sent the children to live with their father in Florida permanently. She now works in Sebring to be near the children.

``Where has he been? Hello?'' said Margaret Zeller, president of the Hillsborough chapter of the National Organization for Women. ``His past history shows he's biased against women.''

Taylor was unavailable for comment Friday.

Michelle Dudek said she doesn't have enough money to appeal the judge's decision.

In December, Taylor sent a woman to jail when she ran crying from his courtroom after he denied her request for protection from a husband she said had raped her.

The Judicial Qualifications Commission, the agency that supervises the conduct of judges, filed formal charges against Taylor last month.

Taylor, who has been on the bench since 1983, might escape penalty. At 69, he is not running for re-election. The charges will be dropped if he retires, a commission spokesman has said.
