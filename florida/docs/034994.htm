Published Tuesday, May 7, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Computerized doll gives teenagers loud, unexpected dose of reality

Associated Press

KISSIMMEE -- The baby's crying woke him up four or five times, and 14-year-old Joey Coffman was tired when he got up to go to school.

But Joey perked up when he packed up the baby -- along with diaper bag, car seat and infant carrier -- and took it to Horizon Middle School and turned it over to his teacher.

The computerized doll, invented three years ago, is one of the newest tactics against teen
pregnancy. Joey's school started using it this year, along with Osceola County's four high schools.

The program called ``Baby, Think It Over'' aims to give students a taste of life with an infant. More than 15,000 of the dolls have been sold to schools and community groups across the country, said Carol Lambert, a spokeswoman for the manufacturer.

A catalog describes the doll, available in Asian, black, Hispanic and white models, as ``adorable, cuddly and programmed to shriek at random intervals all day and all night.''

Joey knew he had to scrap his after-school basketball game when it was his turn to take the doll home last week. But he thought he might get in a little time on his family's computer before dinner.

But the sound of a crying infant filled the house soon after he arrived home. And it got worse.

The faux-child was cranky.

``It gets kind of frustrating,'' Joey said.

A programmed microprocessor in the doll's back makes it cry unexpectedly. A key students wear on their wrists can stop the unnerving crying -- but not always right away. Students sometimes must keep the key in the doll's back for 20 or 30 minutes to simulate how long it would take to feed a real infant.

Horizon uses the doll in its eighth-grade family and consumer science class. Each student keeps the doll for 24 hours, taking it with them wherever they go.

``It's a much more meaningful experience than just reading about it,'' said teacher Maria Rodriguez. ``That doll has made some of them change their opinions.''

It did for Heather Kester, 13.

``I thought it was going to be easy,'' said Heather. ``Then when it started crying . . .''

Roused from a sound sleep before dawn, Heather spent nearly 30 minutes sitting with the crying doll.

``I thought when I was like 18, I'd have a baby. But uh-uh,'' she said. ``When I have a real baby, I'm not going to be going to school.''
