Published Tuesday, May 7, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Use of drugs, tobacco rising among children, UF professor reports

Associated Press

GAINESVILLE -- Marijuana use has increased dramatically, hallucinogenic drugs are growing in popularity, and cigarette smoking is the highest ever reported among young people, a survey of nearly 200,000 American schoolchildren revealed Monday.

``These findings are alarming and should convince us to focus efforts at middle school prevention and at reinvigorating parents' groups, national education and prevention activities,'' said Mark Gold, a professor of neuroscience, psychiatry, community health and family medicine at the University of Florida medical school, who analyzed the study.

Children are much less fearful of illicit drug use or smoking than they were just a few years ago and are using more potent and more dangerous drugs at an increasingly younger age, the study indicated.

Students are using tobacco and marijuana more frequently because the drugs are inexpensive, easy to obtain and glamorized in the media. Peer disapproval also is low.

The results were based on an annual survey of sixth- through 12th-grade students in 32 states from California to New York. Nearly 200,000 students were polled anonymously on their drug habits and their perceptions of drug use during the 1994-95 school year.

The findings reflect similar recent studies of drug use by America's youth.

Gold analyzed responses in conjunction with the Atlanta-based Parents Resource Institute for Drug Education, of which he is a board member, and presented his findings Saturday at a meeting of the Society of Biological Psychiatry in New York.

One-third of all high school seniors said they smoked marijuana in the past year, and one-fifth smoke at least monthly. Marijuana use increased more dramatically than any other illicit drug.

Other recent surveys have shown that daily marijuana use now exceeds daily alcohol use among high school seniors, Gold said.

The rate of cigarette smoking among sixth- through 12th-graders also has soared, Gold said.

Annual cigarette use reported for middle school and high school students was the highest ever. Nearly one in three sixth- through eighth-graders reported smoking cigarettes annually. And 44 percent of the high school students who responded said they had smoked during the past year, compared with 37 percent in a 1988 sample.

For the fourth consecutive year, hallucinogenic drug use increased among students enrolled in grades six through 12. Hallucinogens include LSD, psilocybin, ``Nexus,'' GHB and other drugs such as Rohypnol.

The rate of hallucinogenic drug use was four to 23 times greater among students who report carrying a gun to school, taking part in gang activities, thinking of suicide often, threatening to harm another and getting into trouble with police, Gold said.

``A permissive atmosphere, drug availability and '60s nostalgia may be leading a generation of young people naive to today's more potent drugs into the mire of addiction and illicit-drug related illnesses,'' Gold said.

The study mirrors a study released last week of 13,000 students by Joseph Califano, former head of the U.S. Department of Health, Education and Welfare, which showed the percentage of eighth-graders using drugs has climbed from 11 to 21 percent since 1991.

That study examined the drug-use habits of students who participated in a Youth Risk Behavior Survey over an 18-month period for the Centers of Disease Control and Prevention.
