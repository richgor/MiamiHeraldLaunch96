Published Friday, May 10, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Court OKs two-thirds tax vote proposal

Super-majority would be needed to change state fees

By JACKIE HALLIFAX
Associated Press

TALLAHASSEE -- A majority vote wouldn't be enough to add taxes to the state Constitution under a proposal the Florida Supreme Court agreed Thursday could go on the 1996 ballot.

Voters will be asked to require two-thirds approval for constitutional amendments changing taxes and fees. The measure itself would need a simple majority.

``This is a pretty good day,'' said David Biddulph of New Smyrna Beach, who leads the group that sponsored the citizen's initiative.

Although taxes and fees generally aren't put in the Constitution, efforts are under way for the second time in recent years to insert a sugar tax to pay for Everglades restoration.

The title of the anti-tax measure is ``Tax Limitation: Should Two-Thirds Vote Be Required for New Constitutionally Imposed State Taxes/Fees?''

In a unanimous unsigned ruling, Florida's high court said the proposed amendment was clearly explained by its ballot title and summary -- the final hurdle to get on the ballot.

The Tax Cap Committee, which has pushed the citizen's initiative, has collected the necessary 429,428 signatures to go before voters.

Although no justice dissented, Justice Ben Overton expressed some reservations in a concurring opinion.

``I believe the public should understand that this provision would prevent a majority of the electorate of this state from changing the tax structure now contained in our constitution,'' Overton wrote.

Voters couldn't decide to increase taxes on the rich or provide tax relief to the middle class and poor without a two-thirds vote, he noted.

It would take a super-majority to place a fee on the sugar industry to pay for Everglades protection or impose a limited income tax in exchange for reducing property and sales taxes, Overton added.

Biddulph believes the measure would make it harder for income-tax supporters to reverse a constitutional ban on the tax.

``Many people believe we should have a state income tax, and we believe this will provide a fire wall against that notion,'' he said.

Justice Harry Lee Anstead concurred with Overton's opinion. Chief Justice Stephen Grimes recused himself from the case.

Florida's high court rejected the initiative in October 1994, when it ruled the measure could not go on the ballot because it dealt with more than one subject.

A month later, voters changed the state Constitution so the single-subject limitation no longer applies to citizen initiatives dealing with taxes or fees.

That change also was pushed by the Tax Cap Committee, which is promoting a proposal to require voter approval of all new taxes and a property rights measure as well.

Biddulph said the super-majority requirement is designed to protect minority groups, such as smokers or a particular industry, from being targeted for tax increases.

``We think that this makes it a much more deliberative decision before we would impose new taxes or fees by constitutional amendment,'' he said. ``We think it's the way to go.''

Two years ago, a group started pushing a proposed amendment that would have levied a tax on sugar to pay for Everglades cleanup. That measure was kept from the ballot by the high court, but backers have started a new campaign.

Eva Armstrong, government relations director of Audubon Society in Florida, said her group questioned the wisdom of the two-thirds rule.

``It's not a fait accompli,'' she said.

Common Cause and 1000 Friends of Florida have filed objections to the proposal.
