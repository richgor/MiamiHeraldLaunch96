Published Sunday, April 28, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Mace may get student booted

Associated Press

PORT ST. LUCIE -- Tiffany Beam's parents had safety in mind when they gave her a can of Mace for protection.

But at Port St. Lucie High School, Mace is considered a weapon, and officials suspended the freshman after finding it in her book bag. Citing the district's ``zero tolerance'' weapons policy, they have recommended the 14-year-old be thrown out of school.

Possession of a weapon on campus merits a 10-day suspension and a recommendation for expulsion, according to the school policy. But the state of Florida doesn't classify Mace as a weapon.

Beam's parents were concerned about her walking to her bus stop in the dark, after hearing about the murder of 9-year-old Jimmy Ryce, who was snatched from his rural Dade County school bus stop. Now they're most concerned about the effect the suspension will have on the girl's education.

``My biggest concern is that this is going to stay on her permanent record,'' Beam's mother, Sally Hurley, said. ``It states `possession of a weapon.' It doesn't say it was Mace or that she never used it or even pulled it out.''

Beam's parents hired an attorney to plead her case before a hearing officer Tuesday and later to the School Board. They hope her good grades and plans for a career in medicine will persuade the School Board to let her stay in school.

``All that we're asking of the School Board is that they use some rationality and common sense regarding weapons,'' attorney Michael Kessler said. ``The school obviously doesn't protect their kids at the bus stops or on their way to and from the bus stops. And we know there are predators out there.''

Jacob Gallmon, executive director for secondary schools, declined to comment, citing rules to protect a child's confidentiality.
