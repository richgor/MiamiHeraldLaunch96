Published Saturday, May 11, 1996, in the Miami Herald.


For the Saturday Clipper

16 killed in crash of plane

By NICK ANDERSON
Associated Press

MEXICO CITY -- A plane carrying a group of miners crashed while trying to land Friday in rugged mountains of northwestern Mexico, killing 16 people and seriously injuring three others, authorities said.

The twin-turboprop De Haviland Twin Otter DHC-6 was flying from Durango, capital of Durango state, when it crashed at 9:30 a.m. (10:30 a.m. EDT) near a remote airstrip, said state government spokesman Manuel Perez.

Perez said both the pilot and co-pilot were killed with 14 others. Three people suffered serious burns and other injuries in the crash in Santa Maria de Otaes, a small mining town about 570 miles northwest of Mexico City.

Witnesses said the plane came to rest in a steep ravine about 100 feet from the runway.

The plane was used by a company that mines silver and gold in the area, Perez said.

Company workers were the first to respond at the scene, authorities said. Army and state police officers, some flown in from Durango, arrived afterward.

Television broadcasts showed bloodied bodies, wrapped in black plastic and red-woolen blankets, being carried off a light plane that airlifted them from the crash site to Durango's airport.

One man was in serious condition and two other men in critical condition at the San Jorge Clinic in Durango, said a spokeswoman at the medical center who asked to remain anonymous.

Authorities had no immediate report on what caused the crash; the weather was clear at the time.

Martha Lopez, a reporter for Televisa TV news, said the plane was on a twice-weekly scheduled flight into Durango state's western Sierra Madre mountain range.

It is a rugged area backed by jagged ridges with such names as the ``Spine of the Devil,'' and a rich mining region producing silver, gold, copper, lead and zinc. It is just inland from Mexico's northwestern Pacific coast.
