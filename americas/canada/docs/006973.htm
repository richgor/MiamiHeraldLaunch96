Published Friday, May 3, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Support fading for contempt charge against Bloc Quebecois MP

By LINDA DROUIN
Canadian Press

OTTAWA -- Attempts by the Reform Party to pin a contempt of Parliament charge on a Bloc Quebecois member of Parliament may be a waste of time for a Commons committee, says Liberal whip Don Boudria.

Committee members must decide whether Jean-Marc Jacob was in contempt when he sent a letter Oct. 26 to Quebec bases encouraging soldiers to join a Quebec army if the separatist side won the referendum.

Both the Liberals and the Bloc seem fed up with the committee process.

The Bloc plans to present a motion when the committee meets again next week demanding that Jacob's ``trial'' be ended immediately.

And Boudria appeared exasperated Thursday, saying Reform MPs can't agree on their accusations.

``It needs to be clarified for me. I don't want to say it's a waste of time, but it's certainly less meaningful than before,'' he said after Jacob appeared for the first time as a witness.

Jacob told the committee he sent the communique because many Quebec soldiers didn't seem to be aware that separatist policy called for a Quebec army after separation and many of them were worried about their jobs.

Jacob said he was trying to reassure them there would be work for them in a Quebec army.

``That I could be accused of contempt of Parliament is exaggerated,'' he said.

Reform MP Jack Frazer told Jacob he never thought his letter was advocating the use of force to bring about Quebec separation.

He later denied his position is at odds with Jim Hart, the Reform MP who first brought the complaint against Jacob.

Jacob probably could not legally be charged with sedition, but his communique was ``inviting people to relinquish their obligation to Canada and transfer it to Quebec,'' Frazer said outside the committee room.

He said the committee must make it clear that it's inappropriate for an MP to send such a communique because there likely will be another referendum campaign.

Jacob's appearance was in sharp contrast to Hart's testimony last week when Hart came under fire from Liberal and Bloc MPs.

Hart complained Thursday that the Liberals on the committee were giving Jacob an easy ride because they're afraid of offending the separatist movement in Quebec.

``As a Canadian, that makes me feel very frustrated,'' he said after Jacob's testimony.

Hart said he still believes that Jacob ``flirted with sedition.'''

He said he's also angry that Jacob, as the Bloc defense critic, has been getting invitations to speak at Royal Military College in Kingston, Ontario, and with visiting foreign dignitaries.

Jacob tabled a speaking invitation he received from the Kingston college less than two months ago and invitations from Argentina and South Africa to meet defense officials visiting Ottawa.

``I'm outraged,'' Hart said.
