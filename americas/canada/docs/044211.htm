The government is seeking to reduce by half the British Columbia fleet.

Canadian fishermen fear plan to save salmon will ruin them

By
Seattle Times

VANCOUVER, British Columbia -- Fishing boats crowd the harbor. On the dock, workers hose down the pier, washing away the pungent odor of fish. But they cannot expunge the smell of fear and anger.

``I think I will be ruined,'' says Vicko Mise, Croatian-born skipper of the commercial salmon boat Rose-Lind. ``My whole life I fish . . . now I think I lose everything, end up broke, on welfare . . . and all because of the government.''

Confronted with the imminent collapse of some wild Pacific-salmon stocks, Canada has launched a plan to reduce by half the British Columbia fleet -- in part, by paying fishermen to voluntarily surrender their licenses. If the plan succeeds, 2,200 boats will be permanently beached and thousands of seasonal jobs lost.

And, desperate to avoid an environmental disaster, officials are also considering a ban on commercial salmon fishing in Canada's western waters. The United States imposed a similar ban in 1993 on Washington state, Oregon and California.

For many fishermen, the extreme measures mean an end to a generations-old tradition of work on the sea. But without aggressive action now, officials on both sides of the U.S.-Canada border say, independent fishermen will vanish along with wild salmon.

Fishermen of both nations are struggling more than ever, caught between boom and bust. A worldwide glut of salmon -- mostly Norwegian and Chilean pen-reared fish -- has driven down prices. Simultaneously, efforts to protect dwindling populations of wild Northwest salmon -- and the genetic diversity they represent -- have meant increased regulation and drastically shortened fishing seasons.

In British Columbia, the commercial salmon fishery employs about 10,000 people and has an estimated worth of $450 million. But Canadian officials say revenues could fall by as much as 40 percent as a result of record low returns to the Fraser and other rivers.

Poor ocean conditions, overfishing -- and in the United States, habitat loss -- have contributed to the unprecedented decline of many West Coast salmon populations.

Because salmon migrate thousands of miles, U.S. and Canadian fishermen compete for many of the same fish. The massive reduction of Canada's commercial fleet is ``a good sign that Canadians are finally prepared to make conservation sacrifices,'' says Don Stuart, a longtime advocate for Washington's salmon fishermen.

Canada's plan does signal a shift in the way it manages the salmon fishery. But whether it will result in long-term conservation remains to be seen. Likewise, it's unclear whether it will lessen the considerable tension over the allocation of fish under the Pacific Salmon Treaty.

Despite assurances from federal Fisheries Minister Fred Mifflin that ``the fish come first,'' Canada's plan to buy back commercial salmon licenses does not ensure fewer salmon will be caught off British Columbia's coast. That's a problem from the U.S. perspective.

``We'll benefit only if Canada reduces the harvest along with the fleet,'' said Rob Zuanich, executive director of the Purse Seine Vessel Owners Association in Seattle. ``If they continue to catch the same numbers, we'll continue to face the same problems.''

If Canada's goal is conservation of salmon, then its plan is flawed -- in part because it's voluntary and, so far, few fishermen are rushing to accept their government's offer.

``Since 1959, I fish here. Now the government wants me to wash my boat and sell it. . . . I will not,'' said John Katnic, skipper of the C.F. Todd, a 72-foot boat equipped with high-tech fish-finding gear and huge nets.

``Maybe I eat scrap fish and cornmeal rest of my life but, so long as I can stay on my feet, I fish.''

Although most Canadian fishermen agree there are too many boats on the water, they're angry the government isn't offering more in return for sacrificing livelihood and lifestyle.

``There's no offer of job retraining, no offer to buy a man's boat. . . . What's a fishermen without a fishing license going to do with a boat? Sink it in the back yard and plant flowers in it?'' asked John Katnich, Vancouver waterfront organizer for United Fishermen & Allied Workers Union. ``Why would anybody sell?''

Officials hope to induce fishermen to sell by putting new limits on their licenses. Instead of entitling fishermen to chase salmon along the length of Canada's west coast, a license will be good for only half the area. Fishermen who can afford it will be allowed to buy a second license and continue to fish the entire coast.

Phil Eby, executive director of British Columbia's Vessel Owners Association, says most fishermen will be priced off the water:

``Before this announcement, a second license cost around $300,000 [Canadian] . . . already that cost is going up. A bidding war has started and, believe me, darn few fishermen can win against the government.''
