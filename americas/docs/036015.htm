Three points of light

IN LATIN DEMOCRACY
Paraguay, Mexico, and the Dominican Republic edge closer to fuller democratic status.

Also last month, Dominican President Joaquin Balaguer appeared to accept the legitimacy of the independent electoral observers who will oversee his country's May 16 presidential vote. This network of election watchdogs has backing from the United States and other foreign governments.

It was set up after widespread fraud marred the 1994 Dominican elections. To avert a national crisis, Dominican parties accepted the mediation of the OAS, which helped midwife the pact that defused the Dominican crisis.

Finally, on April 15, at last the Mexican government presented a set of political reforms that, despite a boycott of the leading opposition party, represent in theory an advance toward the transformation of the Mexican state that President Ernesto Zedillo has long promised. The reforms center on making it easier for ordinary Mexicans to initiate petition drives for the purpose of holding national referendums; on giving all national political parties more equitable access to the media; and on granting genuine independence to the national Electoral Commission.

The health of Latin American democracy may not be as robust as the optimists believe. But as these three April examples indicate, neither is it as dispiriting as the pessimists suggest.
