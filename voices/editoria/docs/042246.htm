Published Thursday, May 9, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Scoring Dade's schools

TEST RESULTS RISE Local students improved on a key national test, but the middle schools' lag is cause for concern.

So the good news is that Dade is improving and, by some measures, exceeding expectations drawn from its demographics. That's a credit to the teachers and administrators -- and to parents who may be poor but care deeply about their children's education.

Now for the bad news: Dade's middle schools, like those elsewhere in Florida, continue to lag national norms. That, in turn, will affect the high school students'
performance a few years hence. Students who don't master highly sequential subjects such as math while in middle school likely will struggle in later grades.

The middle schools' problem isn't hard to diagnose. They bear the brunt of the crowding in a very crowded system. The earlier grades are somewhat protected by state laws limiting student-teacher ratios there. High schools, too, have some protection, thanks to the accreditation process.

As a result, the middle schools are targeted when the school board raises the pupil-teacher ratio in order to pay for larger salary increases than the Legislature funded. The formula: Hire fewer teachers so as to pay each of them more.

Thus the student-teacher ratio in Dade's middle schools topped 23:1 last year vs. 18:1 for elementaries. In practice, this means lots of classes with more than 40 students -- and not just any students: Middle schools -- grades 6 through 8 -- enroll squirmy kids in the throes of adolescence. No wonder teacher turnover is highest there.

So these test results, though welcome, aren't a clean bill of health. Rather, they're also useful as a diagnostic tool. The prognosis: Unless something is done soon to ameliorate the problems in Dade's faltering middle schools, the promising gains in the elementary pupils' scores could easily be squandered and lost.
