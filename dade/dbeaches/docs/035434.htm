North Beach businesses are getting rejuvenated

2 hotels undergoing $1.5 million face lifts

By RICK JERVIS
Herald Staff Writer

Manny Hagen remembers better days at his hotel.

He remembers men in suits as crisp as fresh money streaming in with their young families and lines that stretched to the street for a room. He remembers visitors from Chicago, New York and Canada cramming the lobby and passing the time on the oversized porch with cards or Chinese mah jong.

It was the 1950s. His parents, aunt and uncle were running the Ocean Surf Hotel in North Beach and times were good.

But times have changed and Hagen has since witnessed economic droughts at the 49-room hotel at 7436 Ocean Ter., including fewer visitors and seasons full of empty rooms.

To bring back the glory days Hagen -- now the hotel's owner -- is pouring half a million dollars into the Ocean Surf, a signal of the buoying confidence stirring in North Beach.

``It's a big undertaking,'' said Hagen, 60, referring to the renovation that will give the hotel new plumbing, bathrooms and air conditioning while preserving its classic Art Deco architecture.

``If we would like for this to be another tourist area like it was when I was a kid growing up, we have to do this,'' he said.

Built in the late 1940s as the Alcan Surf Hotel, the Ocean Surf was bought by the Hagen family in 1951 and has been family-owned and operated ever since.

The Ramada Deauville Hotel, 6701 Collins Ave., is also making facade and ballroom renovations costing nearly $1 million. Eckerd Drugs at 7400 Collins Ave. is expanding, and a Blockbuster Video store is moving into 65th Street.

Why all the activity?

North Beach property owners are encouraged by the $34 million in improvement projects being managed by the North Beach Development Corp.

A $20 million streetscape project under way will improve drainage and lighting and beautify medians throughout the area.

Sidewalks at Ocean Terrace will be 10 feet wider than the sidewalks on Ocean Drive, hopefully paving the way for sidewalk cafes along the three-block oceanfront stretch between 73rd and 75th streets.

``The rest of the city is very quickly going to recognize the fact that North Beach is a very special place,'' said Ronnie Singer, executive director of the North Beach Development Corp. ``The next few years are going to be tremendous in terms of growth and in terms of desirability. And Ocean Terrace is going to be a major destination spot.''

Renovations to the Ocean Surf, to begin in June, will close it down for four to six months, said Brian Kraus, Hagen's son-in-law and director of operations. The improvements will also widen doorways and install entrance ramps to accommodate disabled visitors, he said.

The improvements were needed and long-coming, Kraus said.

``We weren't going to do anything until the city did,'' Kraus said. ``Now we see a big future here. A lot of things are happening in this area.''
