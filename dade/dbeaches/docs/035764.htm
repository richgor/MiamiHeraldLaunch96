Published Sunday, May 5, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Designer deception: Police pounce on phony shirts

By DAMARYS OCANA
Herald Staff Writer

Just fake it.

That's what Metro-Dade police say the owner of a Doral wholesale manufacturing company was trying to do. They arrested him Thursday for allegedly making and selling knock-off top name T-shirts -- including Nike and Tommy Hilfiger.

Police confiscated $10,000 worth of the shirts in a scenario that has become more and more familiar.

Police say Jorge Murcia, 37, of Embroidery USA, 7064 NW 50th St., used stolen computer disks programmed with the brands' logos to the sew the designs on T-shirts and jerseys of lesser quality.

The list of the company's fake shirts reads like a who's who of brand names -- Polo, Guess, Hard Rock Cafe. The company was even making ``Olympics '96'' T-shirts, police say.

The fakes look like the real thing to a consumer, but investigators from A Action Investigators and Security, a private company that regularly checks out local shirt manufacturers for big-name trademark clients, found torn off original tags and average-quality fabric, police said.

Also missing -- a ``licensed product'' tag.

Investigators bought samples from Embroidery USA's showroom and sent them to the real manufacturers to confirm whether they had made the clothes. They said no and told investigators to call police.

``You pay top dollar for these things and don't get what you pay for,'' said Lt. Mickey Brelsford, of Metro-Dade's Doral District station. ``In some cases you can see where the tag's been sewn on top of the original one.''

Police do not yet know who sold Murcia the computer logo programs or the codes necessary to operate them. It is also unclear who Murcia was selling the shirts to, but police said they are often sold at flea markets or exported to South and Central America.

Exporting has made the business of making and selling fake clothes a booming one in Miami, investigators say.

``When you counterfeit goods you are taking away the jobs of manufacturing workers,'' said an undercover investigator who did not want to be named. ``It's only getting bigger and bigger.''

It costs U.S. manufacturers an estimated $2 billion a year in lost business, he said.

Murcia has been charged with two felonies: forging or counterfeiting private labels and vending goods with counterfeiting labels.

Police said Embroidery USA is still open for business, as long as it is the legal kind.
