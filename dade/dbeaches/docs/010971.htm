Published Sunday, May 5, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Young actors are choice of critics

Teens win honor for performance

By CRISTINA M. ORTEGA
Herald Writer

Students from Miami Beach High won the Critics' Choice award in ensemble acting at the Florida State Thespian Festival in Tampa. They performed a scene from the play Latins Anonymous, which deals with Hispanic stereotypes in movies and plays.

``When we won I yelled louder than any yell ever heard. I'm so excited,'' said Gary Graff, the theater arts director at Beach High.

Now the students have the chance to compete in the International Thespian Festival at the University of Nebraska in Lincoln June 25-30. The festival will feature guest speakers and performers, acting workshops and various college representatives.

Graff said lack of money is the only thing holding them back. The students need to raise $4,000 by May 20 -- and they are planning various fund-raisers, such as car washes and a dinner theater. They will also accept donations, Graff said.

``We're hoping and praying we can raise the money and represent Beach High at the competition,'' said Mia Cruz, 17, a junior.

The five-minute scene depicts four actors auditioning for a role in a play: Pablo Martini, 17, plays a Mexican named Chano; Mia is Pablo's Mexican girlfriend named Maria; Ananta Marra, 18, plays a Puerto Rican named Chino; and Maritza Morales, 18, is Marra's Puerto Rican girlfriend also named Maria. Joseph Renghini, 18, plays the director who is auditioning the four actors.

``The play is saying something about stereotypes in a funny way,'' Morales, 18, said. ``In movies all Hispanic women are Maria and all Puerto Ricans are called Chino.''

Morales, a junior, said the characters try to ``out Hispanic'' each other. The Mexican Maria ends up shooting the Puerto Rican Maria because the Puerto Rican Maria is louder and sassier than she is.

``In the play, I'm covering her up. I'm out-staging her, so she kills me,'' Morales said.''

Chino and Chano also end up dead after getting into a fight over whose performance was better, more Hispanic. The scene ends with the director telling the actors they need heavier Hispanic accents.

The students rehearsed the scene for about two months before taking it to the state competition, which attracted more than 3,700 entries -- about 75 of them were in ensemble acting.

``We were the last audition of the day. So we all thought the judges just would want to get out of there and finish,'' Cruz said. ``But they loved it. They couldn't stop laughing.''
