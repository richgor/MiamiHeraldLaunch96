Investigation continues
into double UM slaying
By FRANCES ROBLES and JOHN LANTIGUA
 Herald Staff Writers

Tucked in Charlie Postell's drawer is a scrapbook, a compilation of photos and clippings of her son's brief football career.

The last entry was a Herald article from Thursday.





GRIEVING: Friends of murdered UM football player, Marlin Barnes, L to R., Lisa Joseph, Lisa Binns and Lisa's sister, Keisha Binns, during speech by UM President Foote at the student union on UM campus.


In the back room of Elizabeth McQueen's house are piles of boxes filled with her granddaughter's belongings. They will probably never be unpacked.

"This is where it stops," Postell said, flipping through the mementos she began collecting when her son wore No. 11 at North Miami High School. "It can't go any further, and look at all these empty pages. When you start something, you're supposed to finish it."

Future articles about her son Marlin Barnes will not be about his linebacker moves for the University of Miami Hurricanes. They'll be about how he and his friend Timwanika Lumpkins were found dead Saturday at Barnes' UM apartment. Beaten to death.

The killer has not been caught. Detectives spent Sunday casing the apartment, carrying out carpeting and furniture from the murder scene -- Apt. 36C.

"I was up all night thinking about who would want to do this to him," Postell said. "I can't think of anyone. He never did anything to hurt anybody."

Lumpkins' family wishes they could say the same.

Her grandmother Elizabeth McQueen said her granddaughter, nicknamed "Little Bit," moved back home about a week ago, saying her boyfriend of nearly four years had roughed her up. Barnes, a friend from North Miami High, helped lug her boxes.

Last week, Lumpkins, 22, told the grandmother who raised her that she was going to get a restraining order against her ex-boyfriend, a rapper from Northwest Dade.

"I told her a restraining order doesn't stop nobody from killing nobody," McQueen said. "You see all these people on TV getting killed with restraining orders? You know what that does? It makes them madder. She never did get it. She didn't have time."

McQueen said Lumpkins' ex-boyfriend, with whom she has a 3- year-old daughter, Antonesha, came to McQueen's South Miami home Sunday to gather clothes for the girl. McQueen wouldn't let him in.

"I asked him what on earth is going on," she said. "He said he wished he knew."

Police were not calling the ex-boyfriend a suspect.

Metro-Dade police spokeswoman Lt. Linda O'Brien said, "He, along with everyone else, is being interviewed. They're talking to people, friends and relatives. Generally, a friend knows a friend, who knows another friend who knows another. Most murders are committed by someone who knows the victim. There's a lot of legwork to be done."

Lumpkins' former boyfriend did not return calls left on his pager and could not be reached at his mother's house.

Police would release no further information.

Lumpkins and Barnes spent their last hours at a Miami Beach nightclub that was hosting a party for the Miami Dolphins and Dallas Cowboys.

Barnes called his mother Friday afternoon, asking if he could use his credit card -- for which she paid the bills -- to buy a new shirt for the party. Lumpkins wore a black satin dress. Before she left, she kissed her grandmother goodbye.

"She looked so pretty," McQueen said.

Barnes and Lumpkins were found dead the next morning by UM safety Earl Little, Barnes' roommate and best friend.

Now two families are reeling.

Barnes' mother sat stone-faced in her Carol City home, surrounded by family and friends. Calls poured in. Former UM football coach Dennis Erickson called. UM President Edward "Tad" Foote came over and embraced her.

Barnes, 22, attended Hargrave Military Academy in Virginia before going to UM. He was a member of Right Trac, a mentoring organization.

His father, Mackey Barnes, was murdered 20 years ago by his best friend. Marlin Barnes grew up in the James E. Scott Homes project in Liberty City.

Lumpkins graduated from North Miami High. She ran track and liked playing basketball. She had a job working with computers, her grandmother said.

"She was the sweetest person you've ever seen in your life," McQueen said. "She never had a frown on her face."

The killings cast a pall Sunday on the university's reception for prospective freshmen and transfer students, who were greeted by an onslaught of media covering the grim news.

Foote spoke to about 1,200 prospective students and their families.

"You are here one day after a terrible tragedy occurred on this campus that took the lives of two young people, one of whom was a student here. It is a very rough moment," he said. "Instead of the enthusiasm and excitement we hoped to welcome you with, you are here on a day of mourning."

He told them that Metro police had three teams of homicide detectives on the case and that campus security had been considerably increased. He later told media that there were about 40 Coral Gables police and private security officers assigned to campus security, at least 15 more than usual.

"Should you decide that despite your interest in this university you should go elsewhere, that is a decision you have to make," he said. "I personally would not make that decision. This is a safe place to live and study."

The prospective students and their families expressed concern, but none interviewed said the killings had convinced them to stay away from UM.

"It's scary, but I can't let it affect me," said Jennifer Chasen, 17, of Boca Raton. "This is where I want to be next year. And I've been on campuses of other schools where there was no security at all."

Her mother, Andrea Chasen, said they had gone to the dormitory where Jennifer will live, found that pass cards with bar codes were needed to enter the building and had spoken with a resident assistant about security.

"I assume the dormitories are more secure than the apartments where the murders happened," she said. "I'm feeling secure about her safety, and I know my daughter is mature enough to take precautions."
