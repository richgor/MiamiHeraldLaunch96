Published Monday, May 6, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Gas pains

Commuters are driving less and car-pooling more

Yolanda Pruneda stared with amazement as the price on the gas pump at a Coconut Grove Shell station kept climbing.

``My God,'' gasped Pruneda, holding her face with both hands. ``It's already over 50 dollars. I can't believe it.''

Pruneda, a Homestead resident, was pumping premium grade gas into one of those gas-guzzling light trucks popular in suburbia.

She was one of about two dozen South Florida commuters who shared stories about how they're dealing with the nationwide gas price hike of 1996.

From Florida City to Boca Raton, commuters are coping. They say they're driving less, car-pooling more or switching to other modes of transportation -- even bicycles.

``A couple of times last week, I car-pooled into the office with co-workers,'' Pruneda said as she pumped gas into a large white Chevrolet Suburban. ``I think twice before I get into my vehicle to go run an errand. Before, I would just do it.''

Ditto for Robert Bobyack, a salesman filling his white Mazda's tank at a Chevron station in Boca Raton.

``I'm doing more work on the phone these days,'' Bobyack said. ``I'm trying to cut down on my driving. I do more planning ahead before I get into the car. I try to combine trips.''

One long-distance commuter, a man who drives once a week between Miami and St. Petersburg, said he's going to start taking the plane because flying may be cheaper.

``With high gas prices and tolls and wear and tear on the car, I estimate it costs me about $50 or $55 per trip in the car,'' said Armand Melanson, a casino boat slot machine technician. ``Flying costs $54, and I save on the wear and tear on the car.''

Joann Heabert, a social worker, also decided to leave her car at home.

``She's biking to work,'' said Paul Heabert, her husband, who was filling up at a Shell station for a holiday road trip. ``But she doesn't work that far from home.''

And he hasn't changed his driving habits and is betting he won't have to.

``I think prices eventually will drop,'' Heabert said.

Other drivers weren't as casual about the price jump.

Miriam Ortiz, a student counselor in Miami Beach, has switched to a lower grade of gas.

Switching to regular grade is actually recommended by the American Automobile Association Clubs of Florida.

``Don't buy premium grade gasoline if your car doesn't need it,'' AAA said in a statement last week. ``The majority of cars are engineered to run on regular [87 octane] gasoline. Motorists who aren't sure of whether their cars need premium grade gasoline should check their owner's manual.''

While most commuters expressed anger, dismay or shock at the prices, a few dismissed the concern as premature or misplaced.

``Yes, there is a gas price increase, but it's not cause for alarm,'' said Placido Herrera, a Coconut Creek resident who commutes to Broward Boulevard.

``People forget that only a few years ago, we were paying really high prices for gas,'' Herrera said.

He's right. Herrera paid $1.49 one day last week for a gallon of premium grade gas for his shiny red Dodge Stealth at a Citgo station at the Florida's Turnpike Pompano Beach service plaza.

That may be the highest price since the Persian Gulf War five years ago. But it pales with the more than $2, adjusted for inflation and taxes, that stations charged between 1980 and 1982.

Unadjusted, gas prices today are still lower than in the 1980s. Florida's average price for self-serve unleaded rose last week 11.1 cents to $1.26 a gallon. In March 1981, the U.S. city average price of regular unleaded stood at $1.41 a gallon.

Herrera has no plans to drive less.

``When you gotta travel, you gotta travel,'' he said. ``Gas prices are like milk prices. You pay them because you have to drink it.''
