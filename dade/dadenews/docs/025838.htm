Day of degrees, day of dreams for 2,351 UM graduates

It's champagne, kisses as 2,351 get UM degrees

By MIRTA OJITO
Herald Staff Writer

Now it's official.

Neville Henry Peter, 23, is a jazz singer.

Cristina Pellerano, 22, is an actress.

And Melenie J. Lankau, 30, is a college professor.

A few words Friday from University of Miami President Edward ``Tad'' Foote made their career choices actual careers, their childhood dreams, reality. It was graduation day.

``This is just the final benediction to send us out into the world,'' Pellerano said, before jumping fully clothed into UM's fountain -- a graduation ritual at the Coral Gables campus.

``New York, here we go!'' And to the fountain she went, drenching her black gown and cap, her white high-top sneakers, and her dainty pearl necklace.

Pellerano is one of only four students who graduated with a degree in the performing arts. Twenty students began the program; 16 dropped out.

``It was tough, tough, tough, but we made it,'' said Jordan White, 22, dripping water.

As the four posed for what could be the smallest class picture ever taken, a festive mood was quickly taking hold.

In front of grinning parents and proud professors, students, now graduates, gulped down $26 bottles of Moet & Chandon champagne as if it were Perrier water. Couples embraced and kissed with the sort of intimacy more common to Champs Elysee than to San Amaro Road.

Minutes before, 2,351 graduates had sat still in a perfectly sunny South Florida morning as Foote and others conferred degrees on them and challenged them to do good.

No one asked more of the students than Sister Jeanne O'Laughlin, president of Barry University and the morning's speaker.

``Be aware,'' she told them. ``Be attentive and take action. Offer welcome and offer hope.''

Some graduates already are much involved in the real world.

Caryn Vogel, a 25-year-old from New Jersey who just received a law degree, will remain in Miami working as a lawyer for the indigent and the sick, said her mother, Carol Vogel, as she wiped a tear or two.

``I'm real proud of her,'' she said.

Peter, the jazz singer who has been blind since childhood, has already performed all over the country and Japan and plans to move to New York to find work.

Lankau, the professor, is the mother of a 4-month-old boy and already has a job teaching management at Cornell University.

Jorge Ramos, a 38-year-old from Mexico who received a master's degree in international studies, will continue doing what he does best: anchoring the news at Univision, the Spanish-language network based in Miami.

Because of his job, it took him three years to finish his studies. Because of his job, he knows firsthand the issues that affect the world.

A few steps from where Ramos sat, his 9-year-old daughter, Paola, kept her eyes glued to the ceremony. At the end, Ramos asked her to throw his cap into the air.

``I wanted her to do this for me,'' he said. ``What better example of sacrifice and effort can I give her?''
