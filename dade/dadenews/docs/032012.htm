Published Sunday, May 5, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

DAY OF JUBILEE

New graduates celebrate with cheers and amens

By DONNA GEHRKE
Herald Staff Writer

Cecilia Winds worked to put 11 children through school. On Saturday, it was her turn.

The 55-year-old grandmother graduated from Florida Memorial College with a bachelor's degree in elementary education.

``It was hard,'' Winds said. ``But I took every obstacle as a steppingstone.''

So did the other 180 students who carried a sheepskin off the podium of the historically black college in North Dade.

Most worked their way through school, with some graduates juggling full-time jobs with night classes. Some had children to tend. Many were the first to attend college in their family.

So when Florida Memorial President Albert E. Smith announced the beginning of the ceremony to hand out diplomas, a roar came up in the packed auditorium.

``We made it,'' senior class President Lexton Symonette exulted in his speech. ``Yes, we did.''

Replied the audience in one breath: ``Amen.''

Hundreds of cameras flashed to record the graduates' short walk to the podium. Anthony Williams, 27, carried off his physical education degree with three generations proudly watching: his 4-year-old son, his girlfriend and his mother, who came from Detroit.

Sen. Bob Graham, the commencement speaker, gave the new grads a dose of optimism: In their lifetime, they'll probably earn double what they would have taken home as high school graduates.

``But with these benefits come responsibility,'' he said. ``One is of leadership. Our task is to build a better society.''

Sherwood Maxine Smith, mother of 4-year-old twins, is already doing that -- as a third-grade teacher at Indian Trace Elementary School in Weston. She won accolades Saturday for graduating with a 3.94 grade point average. Her only B was an American history class. Mischievously, she took a picture of the history professor who kept her from a perfect record.

``I just feel blessed,'' she said. ``I worked so hard.''

So did Demetrius Washington and Carlos Westmore, psychology majors who graduated despite getting suspended once and working to support themselves.

``They said we couldn't do it,'' Washington said. ``And here we are.''
