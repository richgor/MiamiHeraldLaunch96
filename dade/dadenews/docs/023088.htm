Published Sunday, May 5, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Dade wins big money from state

But spending on poor is slashed

By KAREN BRANCH
Herald Staff Writer

TALLAHASSEE -- As a traditional drop of a handkerchief marked an end to the legislative session at 2 a.m. Saturday, there was jubilation and relief on the faces of those who represent Dade's interests.

Miami Heat lobbyist Billy Rubin was radiant.

At the last possible moment, an economic development plan passed. It has a $60 million sales tax rebate to help build a new arena for the team.

Education lobbyists especially were smiling.

``We're better off than last year,'' said Deputy Superintendent Tom Cerra of the Dade County public schools, which get a 2 percent spending hike.

The losers: those who receive social services. Such spending took a $180 million hit statewide to beef up education dollars in a no-new-taxes budget.

Anita Bock, chief of the Health and Rehabilitative Services district that covers Dade, estimates that the cuts will translate into big Dade losses:

  • $1.28 million from health services.

  • $1.7 million from community mental health spending.

  • $2.4 million from children's and family programs.

  • $6.8 million from developmental services for the disabled.

  • $8.5 million from costs to administer welfare programs.

    ``The most devastating cut by far is the one to people with disabilities,'' Bock said. ``That's astonishing in a time when they're telling us we should take people out of institutions.''

    But the budget is also liberally peppered with money for Dade: for colleges and universities, for parks and cultural groups, for meals for the elderly and roads.

    It didn't hurt that Dade has lawmakers in powerful places.

    Sen. Mario Diaz-Balart, R-West Dade, is Senate Ways and Means chairman. Reps. Beryl Roberts-Burke, D-Miami, and Rudy Garcia, R-Hialeah, hold leadership posts on the House budget-writing committee.

    One big-ticket item that Dade lawmakers and lobbyists brought home: $1,375,000 for a new juvenile justice assessment center.

    But Dade can be most thankful that annual budget tussles pitting urban school districts against rural ones didn't hurt the county. Dade public schools will have $95.5 million in new money.

    They also may get a chance to use lottery dollars to build new schools.

    Late Friday, lawmakers passed a bill spearheaded by Diaz-Balart and Garcia. It says districts can take 5 percent of their lottery money each year for five years and use it for school construction bonds.

    In Dade, five years of lottery income adds up to $20 million.

    That would get the district about $100 million in bonds, enough to build 50 new schools.

    But before Dade can use the money -- or any money in the new budget -- the proposal must make it past the desk of Gov. Lawton Chiles.

    Chiles' budget director Bob Bradley isn't so sure it will.

    ``Lottery money is not necessarily the best source because legislators change the way it's spent from year to year,'' Bradley said. ``We have to see how national bond-rating agencies view that as a bonding source.''
