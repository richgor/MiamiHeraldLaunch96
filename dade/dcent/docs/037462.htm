Published Thursday, May 9, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Threats rattle UM student

Israeli activist target of campaign

By ELAINE DE VALLE
Herald Staff Writer

Someone keeps threatening an 18-year-old student at the University of Miami, possibly because of her ties to Israeli and Jewish causes.

Shelly Sitton found a note last week under the windshield wiper of her 1989 Jeep Cherokee when she left the school's Hillel Jewish Center, where she works part time. It said: ``Ready to go boom?''

It was a false alarm but another in a series of threats Sitton has received the past few weeks.

When Nation of Islam minister Rasul Hakim Muhammad came to speak on campus March 28, Sitton helped organize a peaceful protest by Jewish students.

Now, she's getting threatening phone calls. Two weeks ago, someone scrawled ``Be careful -- we are watching you'' on the door of her dorm room. It got scary, she said, when a Muslim student confronted her on campus.

``He was walking behind me and started making racial and ethnic slurs and anti-Israeli slurs,'' Sitton said. ``He was making threatening remarks.''

She recognized him from Israeli-Arab forums she has helped organize for the center, but she doesn't know his name.

Rabbi Jaime Aklepi, director of the Hillel Jewish Center, said Sitton is one of the more active students. ``She's vocal. She's one of our more vocal students in Jewish causes and the causes for Israel.''

University administration and Coral Gables police are taking the situation seriously, said Police Chief James Butler.

``During the end of the school year, we usually have a lot of bomb threats to disrupt testing. But this one is singling out a student,'' Butler said. ``It's a legitimate threat.''

Butler has assigned a detective to the case. ``Right now, it's a bomb threat. It'll turn out to be a hate crime if it turns out to be for religion,'' he said.

That's what the victim and her family think. They contacted the U.S. State Department and the Israeli Consulate to report the incidents. Sitton is not following her regular routine.

``If it was just a one-time occurrence, I would have been more careful but I wouldn't be so concerned,'' Sitton said. ``But because obviously someone has been at my building and knows where I live, and knows my parents' home number and where I work, I started taking more drastic security measures.

``Things are different.''

But the triple major -- who is studying biology, political science and Judaic studies -- said she won't stop her activities with the Hillel center.

``I wouldn't give them the satisfaction. I'm not going to stop doing anything. That's exactly what they want.''
