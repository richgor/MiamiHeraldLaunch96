Published Thursday, May 9, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

To politicians bearing signs, he is the tear-or of Old Cutler Road

By CHARLES RABIN
Herald Staff Writer

As long as people continue to place signs along historic Old Cutler Road, William Lain Guthrie will be right behind them, ripping the signs down and throwing them away.

``To be blunt about it, people are stealing from me,'' Guthrie said, claiming the property value of his five acres on the southeast corner of Southwest
144th Street and Old Cutler Road drops every time ``that garbage'' is put up.

Last month's Pinecrest election was about all the 83-year-old could handle.

The village's 24 candidates had so many signs along Old Cutler between Kendall Drive and Southwest 136th Street that Guthrie went on a daily tear, pulling down whatever ones he could reach and tossing them into the back of his Volkswagen.

It almost got him in trouble.

One day, just prior to the election, Guthrie was going through his usual daily grind of taking down signs along the Old Cutler right-of-way when a police officer pulled him over.

Guthrie said the officer detained him for 37 minutes, told him to toss his signs on a nearby sidewalk and threatened him with the Baker Act -- which allows police officers to immediately restrain someone they suspect of mental incompetence.

``I told him anyone who thinks he can keep Dade County's streets clean ought to have his head examined,'' Guthrie said.

The officer eventually let Guthrie go.

During the same campaign, Guthrie called soon-to-be Pinecrest Councilman Barry Blaxberg and accused him of placing signs along bus benches on Old Cutler.

Blaxberg wrote Guthrie back, telling him he thought the signs were ugly but necessary.

``It was a race keeping up with him,'' said Blaxberg, who tried to keep replacing the signs Guthrie kept pulling down. ``I admire his goal. If we can centralize signage for elections, that would be wonderful. But I don't believe in people unilaterally taking the law into their own hands.''

Guthrie has been fighting to keep Old Cutler clean for a long time. He built and moved into his home in 1944. ``I thought it was beautiful then, and I want it to be beautiful now,'' he said.

In 1993 he sued Metro government to have the county purge Old Cutler Road of all offending signs and traffic signals.

``He's spending a lot of money on what he feels is a just cause,'' Max Price, Guthrie's attorney, said at the time. ``I think Lain feels that if he doesn't do it, nobody will.''

The case is supposed to reach court this summer, Guthrie said.

Dealing with Old Cutler isn't the first time Guthrie has faced powerful opposition. In 1970, Eastern Airlines fired him as a pilot when he refused to dump jet fuel. He fought his bosses and got his job back.
