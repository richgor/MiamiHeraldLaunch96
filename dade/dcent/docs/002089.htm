Published Sunday, May 5, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Volunteers honored for guiding kids in reading and writing

By SABRINA WALTERS
Herald Staff Writer

Dr. Jean Perdue treated hundreds of patients at her Miami Beach office. But now she has refined her practice to reading, writing and arithmetic.

Perdue, 93, works with elementary students through Senior Mentors for Creative Students Program.

``One great gift the older person can give to the community is helping a child to read and write,'' said Perdue, who is also a board member of the Dade chapter of the Alliance for the Aging. ``There isn't enough money and teachers, so that's where we come in.

``A child who gets to the third and fourth grade and can't read or write, they're in serious trouble.''

Perdue and five other volunteers -- Evelyn Best, Beverly Hammel, Thomas Reed, and Hy and Joan Rosner -- will be honored for their work Thursday at the Radisson Mart Hotel.

The Senior Mentor for Creative Students Program started 10 years ago as an offspring of the Adopt a Grandparent program, in which senior citizens are paired with young students. Today, nearly 3,200 senior citizens mentor 5,000 Dade students.

``You think there's an age generation gap, but when you see them together, it's almost an instant bonding,'' said Ramona Frischman, supervisor of intergenerational programs for Dade schools.

``They really find they have a lot in common. They're both discriminated against because of age. Either they are too old, or too young.''

Students at Centennial Middle found a lot in common with Thomas Reed, 59, a retired New York City health administrator.

Reed, who studied music at Indiana University and a member of the Florida International University Master Choral and the Civic Choral of Greater Miami, took his voice skills to Centennial.

``There was a group of kids who wanted to start a choir, but they didn't have an instructor,'' Reed said.

In January, Reed began giving voice lessons and introducing the students to a variety of music, including spirituals and folk songs. Since then, the Centennial choir has competed in local and state events.

Hy and Joan Rosner, former New York educators, have spent the past 12 years traveling between Miami and New Mexico, sharing a save-the-environment message with children in both places.

In Dade, the couple has organized overnight nature trips for elementary school students, written a book about the history of Miami's environment, and brought students face-to-face with scientists to study pollution.

``When you retire, that doesn't mean you have left your brain behind,'' said Hy Rosner. ``We believe in trying to make the places we stay better than how we found it when we got there.''

Retired Dade teacher Beverly Hammel, 69, was so inspired by the mentor program at M.A. Milam Elementary in Hialeah that she rounded up a dozen residents at nearby Epworth Village -- former teachers, business owners and an attorney -- to also help.

``I said, `Let me get these people and get them over to school,' '' Hammel said.

Evelyn Best, 71, is being honored mentoring and recruiting parishioners from her church to help at Jackson High.
