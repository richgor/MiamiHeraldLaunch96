Published Wednesday, May 8, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

State launches probe into police shooting Hallandale incident `unusual'

State to probe police shooting

Man was hurt
in Hallandale


By FRANK FERNANDEZAND TRISH POWER
Herald Staff Writers

The Florida Attorney General's Office on Tuesday launched an investigation into the apparently accidental shooting of a Davie man by a Hallandale police officer.

``The circumstances appear suspicious,'' said Gregory Durden, director of civil rights for the Attorney General's Office. ``This is a little unusual because he was not armed. He was complying with what she wanted. There was no reason for him to get shot.''

Hallandale police maintain the man was armed with a knife -- though they won't say where the knife was found after the shooting.

``We've had about 500 different stories as to what was going on,'' said Capt. James Kirchoff. ``It's still under investigation.''

This much is clear:

Donnell Mitchell, 37, was shot in the buttocks as he lay face down on the ground Monday evening.

He had been ordered there by officer Marsha Roaden -- who had been summoned by a city worker to investigate some kind of fight.

Roaden was using one hand to handcuff Mitchell as she clutched her gun in the other.

``Just think about it. How are you going to handcuff somebody if you have one hand with a gun in it?'' Durden said.

Witnesses said the problem started when Mitchell was attacked by a group of people while riding a bicycle along Northwest Sixth Avenue. On Tuesday, residents of the quiet Northwest Hallandale neighborhood said Mitchell had stolen the bike. The fight was a result of some residents trying to reclaim it. Police couldn't verify the story.

Mitchell's criminal record includes convictions for arson and criminal mischief. He was sentenced in 1989 to 3 1/2 years in state prison but was released after seven months. He is on probation until May 2001.

He was not arrested Monday. Mitchell refused to comment Tuesday on the advice of his lawyer.

While onlookers reacted with outrage to the shooting Monday evening, on Tuesday neighborhood leaders called for calm -- but demanded an investigation.

``We realized that basically that was a mistake [Monday],'' said John Hardwick, president of the Northwest Hallandale Civic Association. ``We need to investigate and be more understanding that mistakes do happen. By the same token, we don't tolerate anyone being mistreated.''

Roaden, a 13-year veteran, has been suspended with pay from her approximately $45,000-a-year job while the shooting is reviewed. She is prohibited from publicly discussing the shooting.

Roaden's weapon -- a .40-caliber Glock -- comes from a family of weapons with a reputation for unintentional firings.

While Metro-Dade prohibits the use of Glocks, the light-weight weapon is popular in many departments, including the Miami Police Department, said Armando Valdes, senior firearms instructor for Miami police.

Glocks used by Miami require eight pounds of pressure to pull the trigger. Five is standard.

``We felt five pounds was a little too light,'' Valdes said.

A Glock adjusted for competition shooting needs only 3.5 pounds to fire, he said.

Even with the less sensitive weapons, Miami officers are trained to keep their fingers off the triggers unless they intend to shoot.

Tests showed that reaction times varied less than a second for finger on-the-trigger and finger off-the-trigger practice shooting, Valdes said.

Another reason for off-trigger training is something called ``sympathetic contractions.'' It happens, Valdes said, when you're startled, when you loose your balance or when you exert yourself.

``We did this experiment where we had an officer hold an egg in one hand, then hold heavy books in the other hand,'' he said.

As the weight of the books increased, the hand holding the egg tightened -- eventually cracking the egg.

``What one hand does, the other hand imitates,'' he said. ``That's why we modified our training.''

Commander George Long, a weapons expert at Broward's Criminal Justice Institute, said the .40-caliber Glock is a popular weapon and no more dangerous than any other semi-automatic.

Recruits at the academy are taught to hold unholstered weapons in two positions: firing position at a target, firing position at the ground.

``It's in order to teach control of the weapon,'' he said. ``You have to know how hard it is to pull the trigger.''

But, Long said, recruits are also taught to put the gun in their holster before trying to handcuff a suspect.
