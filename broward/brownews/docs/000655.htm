Published Sunday, May 5, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Teen threatened family, brother says

By CONNIE PILOTO
Herald Staff Writer

Hours before Denovan Johnson allegedly stabbed his mother to death with a kitchen knife, the 16-year-old punched his older brother in the face and threatened to kill the family in their sleep, his brother said Saturday.

But no one thought Denovan would follow through on his threat, Michael Johnson said, sitting in the tiny one-bedroom duplex he shared with his parents and brother.

``My father said not to worry, that he was just talking and wasn't going to do anything,'' said Michael, 18.

Denovan -- who had been in state care for two years -- returned home in February after running away from a local facility, said Department of Health and Rehabilitative Services spokeswoman Lynette Beal.

The first day Denovan stepped inside the east Hollywood duplex, trouble started again.

His parents, Elaine and Henry Johnson, had trouble rearing Denovan, who often was physically abusive. As Denovan grew older, bigger and angrier, problems escalated.

In 1994, HRS took custody of the teen. Denovan was sent to out-of-state facilities: first in Ohio, then Georgia, then Texas.

At the Dartmouth Behavioral Mental Health Center in Dayton, Ohio, Denovan set a fire, Michael said. From there he was taken to a facility in Georgia and then to High Frontier Home in Texas. The ranch-type home near Fort Davis treats youths with intense psychological problems.

His mother wanted Denovan placed in a similar facility where the family could visit.

``He needed family contact,'' Michael said. ``I spent about two years without seeing him. He'd send letters about how tall he had grown and about wanting to play basketball with me.''

Later, Denovan was transferred a less restrictive facility called the Dan Marino Home for Boys in Pompano Beach. There, he went to school in the morning and worked at Burger King.

In February, Denovan ran away and knocked on his parents' front door. They called HRS and told the agency Denovan was home. Weeks later, social workers asked a judge to let him live at home, although he was still under custody of the state, Beal said.

Social workers were supposed to provide counseling for the family but the teen didn't want it, Michael said.

Instead, Denovan spent his days asking for money to gamble at a horse track.

His mother -- who quit her job at a Winn-Dixie because she couldn't handle the family problems -- gave him $500. His brother, who has part-time jobs at Alamo Rent A Car and Winn-Dixie, gave him $200.

He wanted more. Denovan would wait for the mail and snatch his father's Social Security check, Michael said. His parents got a post office box.

Thursday, Denovan punched his brother in the face because Michael wouldn't give him money, then threatened to stab the family, Michael said. Michael went off to work. Denovan hopped on a bus.

Later, Denovan returned home and argued with his mother, police said, then fatally stabbed her with a steak knife. He was charged with murder and is being held in a detention facility.

``Now, I'm scared he's going to get out and kill me or my father,'' Michael said. ``He told my father that when he gets out of jail he'll come back and kill us.''
