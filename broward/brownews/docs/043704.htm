Published Sunday, May 5, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Pines parents, board angered by Weston's K2 program

By SUE CORBETT
Herald Staff Writer

Debbie Wasserman Schultz: Your name is mud in parts of Pembroke Pines.

And now would not be the time to ask the School Board for a favor.

Wasserman Schultz, a state representative whose district includes a wide swath of West Broward, was the architect of a plan to use $270,000 of lottery funds -- earmarked to reduce classroom size -- to build a kindergarten/teen center in Weston.

By day, 5-year-olds from Country Isles Elementary would learn their ABCs there, averting the imposition of half-day kindergarten.

Come midafternoon, middle school students from the K2 Project would make the place their own.

But parents in western Pembroke Pines, whom Wasserman Schultz also represents, want to know why there was no plan for their kids.

``It's really nice what she did for Weston, now what is she going to do for us?'' asked Susan Meister, chairwoman of Chapel Trail Elementary's school advisory council. ``Our overcrowding is worse.''

Wasserman Schultz says she pushed the Weston plan because Arvida, the Weston developer, was willing to kick in $465,000 for construction.

``Had this idea originated in Pembroke Pines, I would have gone to bat for them, too,'' she said Friday. ``I support any effort to reduce overcrowding.''

But Wasserman Schultz also has angered School Board members, who feel she circumvented their authority and question how a facility could be built to house little kids and preteens.

``I have lots of questions and no answers,'' said School Board chairwoman Lois Wexler. ``But the biggest one is, `Am I ever going to get to vote yes or no on this?' ''

Maybe not. The $270,000 appropriation, passed by the Legislature early Saturday, must be matched with $250,000 in private funds by Sept. 1.

No problem, says Dee Scott Valdez, the force behind the proposed teen center, called the K2 Project. There's no money yet, but Valdez plans to raffle off a robot her students are building.

The first thing you need to know about K2 is that it has nothing to do with elementary school.

``When Debbie called me about K2, I thought she was talking about kindergarten through second grade,'' said Diana Wasserman, the School Board member whose district covers Weston and Pembroke Pines.

In fact, the project refers to K2, the world's second-tallest mountain -- because the centerpiece of the center is to be a rock-climbing wall.

But it could also refer to Valdez's uphill struggle to get the center built.

The mother of two adolescents herself, Valdez began seeking support for an after-school program for Weston youngsters three years ago. She went to Arvida's Roy Rogers, who agreed to donate a band shell behind the Weston Athletic Club.

A prominent architect drew plans to enclose and expand the band shell into a 7,500-square-foot building. His design included a computer lab, study hall, game room, offices, soda fountain, in-line skating rink, DJ booth and the climbing wall.

After school, mostly volunteers would use the building to offer homework mentoring, art classes, a ``mad scientists'' club, a baby-sitting referral service, gardening and, of course, rock-climbing.

She wrote an exhaustive business plan for the project, estimating it would cost $694,000 to build the facility.

She projected a need for a staff of four, including a program director and a director of the nonprofit K2 Corp., who would earn $35,000 and $30,000 a year respectively. The second-year operating budget calls for the addition of a public relations director at a salary of $30,000 a year.

To sustain the projected annual operating budget of $182,000, Valdez estimated she would need 600 members to pay between $200 and $400 in dues a year.

Valdez says K2 has 30 to 50 members. Growth is stymied by the lack of a place to meet.

``We have a robotics class that has been meeting out of my garage for the last three months,'' Valdez said.

Though no plans have been drawn, Valdez thinks the center could be reworked to accommodate kindergartners. She envisions round tables that could be rolled to storage in the afternoon, pull-down desks for teachers, blackboards on wheels.

``Ambitious,'' Superintendent Frank Petruzielo calls the plan.

Unworkable, say Country Isles parents.

``Aren't these children going to have desks and chairs?'' asked Shelley Eichner, a Country Isles parent. ``Is somebody going to move those out each day and then move them back?''

Administrators also questioned Valdez's cost estimates. Petruzielo said furnishings alone could run $400,000.

Valdez still thinks there's room to negotiate.

``I care about Weston, so I said, `We'll try to accommodate this,' '' Valdez said. ``We aren't the bad guys. But we might be better off just waiting a year and raising our own money.''
