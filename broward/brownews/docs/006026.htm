Published Sunday, May 5, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

WHAT PASSED, WHAT FAILED

Here is how Broward-related issues fared in the legislative session that ended at 2 a.m. Saturday in Tallahassee. Bills that passed still need Gov. Lawton Chiles' approval before becoming law.
PASSED

Annexation: Future annexations in Broward will need approval of the legislative delegation, but won't require approval by the annexing community in a referendum. Also, Dania and the county agreed to a land swap and an end to a long-running dispute over runway expansion at Fort Lauderdale-Hollywood International Airport.

Fishing museum: The International Game Fish Association gets a $15 million rebate of sales taxes to help build a new world headquarters in Dania on land adjacent to a Bass Pro sports superstore.

Fireworks: Operators of commercial fireworks displays must comply with a national fire safety code, a response to a fatal fireworks explosion on the Pompano Beach pier in 1994.

Maternity stays: Mothers and newborns can count on health-care coverage in the hospital and at home for as long as a doctor considers ``medically necessary.'' A growing trend known as ``drive-through deliveries'' means mothers often go home within 24 hours.

HMO ombudsman: Patients of health-maintenance organizations can complain to a statewide ombudsman committee or one of 11 regional citizen ombudsman boards on the quality of HMO care.

Hair testing: Hair testing could be used as an alternative to urinalysis to test for drugs in the workplace, a change that helps sports mogul H. Wayne Huizenga, a major shareholder in a hair-testing company.

Poker rooms: Bettors at horse and dog tracks and jai-alai frontons can bet up to $10 a pot on card games. The city of Hallandale, the only city with two tracks, will receive an estimated $250,000 a year in revenue.

State butterfly: The zebra longwing butterfly -- a popular attraction at Butterfly World in Coconut Creek -- is an official state symbol.

Weston-Bonaventure: Weston residents will vote on cityhood in a referendum Sept. 3; residents of Bonaventure will vote on April 1, 1997, whether to join Weston or Sunrise.
FAILED

Abortion: Doctors will not be required to delay performing abortions for 24 hours while they explain the risks and alternatives of an abortion to pregnant women.

Bowling: Bowling centers would have faced less risk to potential lawsuits by warning bowlers of the inherent dangers in the popular game.
Gambling: ``Cruises to nowhere,'' or offshore gambling ships, which have multiplied in Broward in the past year, may continue to operate off the Florida coast.

Smaller districts: Voters will not have a say on a proposed amendment to the Constitution to break up large countywide school districts into smaller districts.

New schools: Lawmakers rejected a bid to have home buyers pay higher taxes on recorded deeds to help pay for construction of new schools, and ease the shortage of Broward classrooms.

Nursing homes: Federal regulations designed to protect nursing home patients would have been the law in Florida at a time when Congress is considering changes to Medicaid.

Seat belts: Police won't be able to stop motorists and write tickets for not wearing seat belts. They must continue to issue seat belt citations only after they discover another traffic violation.

Underage smokers: Minors caught possessing cigarettes would have faced $25 fines and driver license suspensions for repeat violations.
