EMERGING FROM SHADOW OF DEATH
BURNED DETECTIVE GOING HOME


By STEPHEN SMITH
Herald Staff Writer

Six months, the doctors murmured to Jim O'Hara.

You're going to be stuck in the rehab ward for six months, bending fingers that refuse to bend, lifting legs that refuse to lift, getting acquainted with a body rendered unfamiliar by the ravage of fire.

Nah, Jim O'Hara demurred in a voice dimmed from lack of use. It won't take half that long.

Know this: Never bet against Jim O'Hara.

"I've learned that I'm a lot stronger than I really thought I was," O'Hara said Wednesday, eight months and 16 days after an inferno ignited by a madman left him desperately burned. "But to be this strong, you've got to have reasons to do it -- my wife and my kids are those reasons."

So there he was, features muted in an explosion that shook a shady suburban street in July. This was the first time the Plantation detective had been bathed in the lights of publicity. He was the one who'd beckoned the cameras to HealthSouth Sunrise Rehabilitation Hospital.

And he shared this news: I'm going home, he told the reporters and doctors and therapists. Friday, I go home, O'Hara said. For good. And it took just eight weeks in the rehab ward.

"Doing this kind of work, the human potential is something I appreciate more and more," said Mick Rathjens, who runs the burn unit at Sunrise Rehab. "But with Jim, I appreciate it more than ever."

The potential shines brightest when the fangs of pain dig deepest. Karl-Henry Gerdes is the physical therapist who has spent the most time with O'Hara, 33, stretching arms and fingers mottled by fire.

"It's something to see him when he's down and tired and even then he says, 'Do it to me one more time,' " Gerdes said. "He says it even if he's in pain."

O'Hara's odyssey of pain began July 25, when he and Plantation officer Joe Alu walked into a bedroom where Steve Joseph held hostage the two teenage daughters of his estranged girlfriend. Joseph lit a bomb brewed with gasoline, incinerating himself and the two girls.

Alu and O'Hara bore the marks of Joseph's insanity, O'Hara worse than Alu. Burns smothered 78 percent of O'Hara's body.

"When I was brought outside, I never thought I was going to make it," O'Hara said. "I was in a lot of pain then. That was really the only time that I can remember being in a lot of pain."

A helicopter ferried him to Jackson Memorial Hospital, where he would spend half a year, with death his constant companion, casting a shadow that seemed to swallow the tough guy cop they call Rocky.

The days and weeks and months drifted by in a gauzy haze, drugs and pain dulling reality. He didn't know that, at the most dire moments, doctors feared that infection would consume him, that his organs would fail him.

Jim O'Hara knew only this: He had a baby son, born six days after the explosion, and he was determined to hold his son, to see his crib in the family's Coral Springs home.

"And a lot of it had to do with God, wanting to stay alive and see my kids, my family," O'Hara said. "It's been a rough eight months, but I'm glad to be here."

O'Hara, clad in olive green khakis and green paisley shirt, was flanked Wednesday by doctors and friends and family. He introduced his 3 1/2-year-old daughter Kelsey and infant son Brett. "No resemblance," O'Hara said, showing a streak of humor that laced through the 21 minutes he spent with media.

He offered tributes to them all but reserved his most profound words for the woman seated to his right, Janine O'Hara.

"The person who brought me through this whole thing was my wife -- without her I never would have made it," O'Hara said. "She's been the strength and foundation of this family.

"I'm just real happy to be alive and see my kids," the detective said, dissolving into tears.

He's been seeing more and more of them -- and more of the world beyond his hospital bed. In recent weeks, there has been a foray to a Jackson Browne concert, a trip to see The Birdcage, a night watching the Panthers.

"He's busier than me," said Gerdes, the physical therapist. "He doesn't stick around here."

That's an essential part of the treatment regimen at Sunrise Rehab. The mind must heal along with the body, so trips to malls and films are encouraged.

"You start to become sort of a recluse if you don't go out," O'Hara said. "You can't be afraid to go out even though your features are remarkably changed."

Someone asked O'Hara about the future Wednesday. I'm not sure what I want to do with the rest of my life, he said. Maybe a return to police work, maybe not.

For months to come, O'Hara will spend part of every weekday at Sunrise Rehab. There's more surgery, too, after surviving more than a dozen procedures.

The therapy, the surgery, those O'Hara can endure. It is, he said, the inevitable nuisances of life that try him most.

"Like, for instance, I got a cold," O'Hara said. "I hated having that cold. After all I've been through, it's like, what else can happen? What else?"
