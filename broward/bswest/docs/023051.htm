Chase? Walter? Crime Stopper? Kids to name police horse

By PATTI ROTH
Special to The Herald

N
icole Sarver, 10, thinks horses can be great partners for police officers.


``They can still get places, and they don't have to pay for gas,'' she said.

Nicole, a fourth-grader from Silver Palms Elementary School, has an idea about a perfect name for a police horse -- Chase.

Thousands of youngsters from Silver Palms, temporarily in Cooper City, and other elementary schools in Pembroke Pines have suggested names for the city's second police horse.

The horses, which are used for patrols and special events, enjoy people and sometimes bow their heads for youngsters to pet them.

Ty, a friendly, chestnut-color horse, has been on patrol with officer Terry Burns for about eight years.

The second horse, a former racehorse, originally had the name Wanna Do It Again.

But that wasn't a simple police-horse name, Burns said.

So the officers agreed to let children help decide on a replacement. After the playful brown-and-white horse toured the elementary schools, the youngsters colored drawings and jotted down name ideas.

The winning name will be chosen by a panel of police officers and officials, and the child who submitted it will be honored May 18 at the Public Safety Department.

``They came up with some names that look like they put thought into it,'' Burns said.

Among the entries were Brownie, Lightning, Cinnamon and Crime Stopper.

Michael Zambrano, a fourth-grader at Pines Lakes Elementary, suggested Walter because he thinks the name is funny for a horse.

He said he's glad the department uses horses because they can sneak up on criminals.

``They're a better way of transportation and less noisy,'' he said.

Andi Caban, 7, of Palm Cove Elementary, suggested Michael -- her cousin's name. Her friend, Ashley Perdomo, a kindergartner, suggested Jonathan.

Andi said she likes the idea of police officers on horseback.

``I think it's great because then you don't have to use a car. Horses go faster,'' she said. ``Horses can go on the sidewalk and keep on running. Cars have to stop at the red light.''

Miriam Eichenbaum, 8, of Pembroke Lakes Elementary School, suggested Brownie because the horse reminded her of a brownie with sugar.

The advantages of the police horse, Burns said, include a broad field of vision for the officers. Also, a horse can maneuver in certain places -- such as between cars -- that automobiles can't.

And horses serve as a way for children and others to meet police officers on a friendly, personal level.

``The horse draws the niceness out of people,'' said officer Ed Klingman, who patrols with the unnamed horse.

Burns agreed.

``They come up and talk to me so they can pet the horse,'' she said.
