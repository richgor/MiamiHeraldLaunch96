SE BROWARD HOMETOWN

Stirling's Earth Day activities offer lessons on environment

E
arth Day will be celebrated a little late this year at Stirling Elementary School, but students will still learn valuable lessons about what they can do to help preserve the environment.

The Hollywood school has arranged for speakers to talk to kids in kindergarten through fifth grade about being sensitive to the environment.

The program started Wednesday, when fifth-graders heard Michael Heimbach talk about recycling programs for the county and Broward public schools.

He challenged the fifth-graders to develop a recycling program for their school.

On May 13, third-graders will hear from Hollywood resident Debra Smiley, who is Mrs. Florida US 1995.

Smiley, an environmentalist and attorney, will present a program about the environment.

Students will learn about the effects of pesticides and how to buy products made from recycled materials.

Fourth-graders will hear from Hollywood recycling coordinator Laurie Mertens on May 13.

Kindergarten and first-graders that day will learn the importance of throwing trash in its proper place by reading the book The Berenstain Bears Don't Pollute (Anymore).
Students at Maimonides Academy tested their knowledge of the city of Jerusalem April 24 as part of their celebration of Israel Independence Day.

The Hollywood school organized a carnival with kids in first through seventh grade creating the game booths, parent Haya Tepper said.

Rather than buy tickets to play a game, students had to answer questions related to Jerusalem, in recognition of the city's 3,000th anniversary.

The carnival also featured a haunted house where students had to search for oil for the Hanukkah menorah, Tepper said.

She said students enjoyed making the carnival booths this year.

``It gave them a real feeling of being responsible for the carnival,'' Tepper said.
Creative Beginnings Preschool is living up to its name by pursuing its goal to become nationally accredited.

The Hollywood preschool is sending a group of its parents on a shopping spree May 18 to purchase new toys and supplies for the school.

Beverly Angelotti, the preschool's owner and director, also decided to add a bit of competition to the activity by seeing which parents could pick the toys out fastest at the store.

``I'm going to say, `Here's your list. You have 20 minutes. Ready, set, go!' '' Angelotti said. ``The parents are into this competition thing, so I know they're going to like it.''

Buying the toys and supplies is part of the school's effort to become accredited by the National Academy of Early Childhood Programs in Washington, D.C.

Angelotti said the accreditation will assure parents that the preschool provides a high-quality program for young children.

The accreditation process has involved a survey of parents and teachers about their views of the school's curriculum and care of the preschool's 100 children, who range in age from infants to 5.

Once the school has met the academy's guidelines, the academy will send a team of educators to observe the school to confirm the findings in Creative Beginnings' application.

Angelotti said she hopes the preschool will receive accreditation by the end of June.

Parents raised $4,000 through a candy and rummage sale in March to buy additional toys and supplies required for the accreditation.

The academy requires that the preschool provide the same age-appropriate toy for each child in the class.

Because parents raised the money, Angelotti decided the parents should be involved in spending it.
