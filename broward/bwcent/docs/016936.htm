Published Sunday, May 5, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

He's friend to youth, elderly

By CONNIE PILOTO
Herald Staff Writer

D
etective Craig Lupo makes a living by sending crooks to jail. But you won't find him running after crooks or following lawbreakers on high-speed chases -- at least, not most of the time.

The Cooper City police detective investigates elderly exploitation and child sexual abuse cases. Lupo listens to the horror stories. Then, he sets off to find the culprit.

``It's a paper chase,'' Lupo said. ``I spend some days following leads and typing supplemental reports.''

Lupo, 34, grew up in Hollywood and graduated from South Broward High School. After high school, he joined the Marine Corps, where he served for four years.

In 1989, he went to the police academy and joined the Cooper City department, where he worked as a patrol officer. Three years later, he was promoted to the detective bureau. Recently, he passed a police sergeant's exam and hopes to be promoted when a sergeant position is available.

Lupo and detective Tom Hewlett earlier this year spent months investigating a case where a 15-year-old girl was allegedly gang-raped after her drink was spiked with sleeping pills known as roofies.

The youths slipped white dime-size pills called Rohypnol -- prescribed as sleeping pills in about 80 countries -- and waited for them to take effect, police said. When the girl dozed off, the three youths took turns raping her numerous times, police said.

``We started getting information from kids at school that the drug was being used and we started investigating,'' Lupo said. ``Then we got a phone call from a victim who wanted to tell us what happened.''
After months of interviews and meetings with the Broward State Attorney's Office, an arrest was made.

Brothers Scott Harmon, 17, and Sidney Harmon, 19, both of Pembroke Pines, and Moises Ventura, 18, of Miramar, were arrested on Feb. 1 and charged with numerous counts of sexual battery. The youths were taken to the Broward County Jail and later released on bail.

``These cases are difficult because there is so much fear,'' Lupo said. ``The victims and witnesses are afraid and the bad guys are usually spreading fear, too.''

Lupo says victims need to trust the police and can't blame themselves for what happened.

``Even if they make a mistake, the kids need to know that no one has a right to violate them,'' Lupo said.
