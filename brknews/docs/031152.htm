Posted at 3:32 a.m. EDT Sunday, May 12, 1996

By Miriam Rosen

My mother's room is at the end of the hall at The Waverly, a boarding school where old moms go to learn their last lesson in family life: how to survive on just their memories.

I enter on quiet shoes, holding my breath. She awakens anyway. Her brown eyes, the only unfaded part about her, blink with pretend amazement.

Her lines are always the same. Like a stage actress reanimating an old script with ironic power: ``Am I dreaming? Are you really here?''

And then (because not too long ever elapses without her bringing up my sister's name): ``I was just talking to Holly about you. We wondered how you are.'' And (because I want her to see things clearly), I say, ``Oh, was she here?'' And (because my mother is at least honest), she says, ``Well, no. You know how busy she is. But we talked on the phone.''

At The Waverly, I'm always irked. My mother used to live in a nursing home 10 minutes from my kitchen. I would start supper, drive over, take the stairs two at a time, butter her roll, tell her about the boys, pull the curtains against the afternoon glare, and get home before the rice stuck to the pot.

Then my sister decided to move mother to Broward to be near her.

So I go less often.

All right. I go seldom.

But oddly, so does my sister.

Even in this low light, my mother looks neglected.

``What's this?'' I ask examining old bloodstains rimming her nails.

``Oh, blood tests,'' says mother folding her hands to distract me.

``And they don't clean it?''

``By this room, they're out of Band-Aids.''

I'm angry as usual in this close room and queasy from the stink of diapers and clouds of antiseptic.

``Did you notice my wheelchair out there?'' asks my mother changing the subject deftly.

I hadn't noticed, distracted by old men sleeping bent and open-mouthed, old women padding in bathrobes and blue vinyl slippers.

``It happened again,'' my mother goes on. ``Two aides ran in and grabbed my wheelchair for an emergency . . . ''

``And didn't bring it back,'' I finish for her.

``And didn't bring it back,'' my mother finishes for herself.

``How often does this happen?''

My mother rolls her eyes.

``Do you tell Holly?''

``I don't like to bother her with things I can handle.''

As I wonder what my mother can handle, she reaches for the phone, dials and waits. My mother is ``doing phone.'' A quality I admire but did not receive. On the phone, my voice goes high and breaks no matter how full of conviction my heart or honest my cause.

My mother's tone is strong, unwavering. ``This is Judith Kane. Once again, Mrs. Winters, floor staff have removed my personal wheelchair from my bedside to rescue a wandering patient.'' She pauses to let this sink in, then goes on. ``The problem is such a commonplace I'm surprised you don't keep a folding wheelchair near the exit for contingencies.''

My mother listens and says, ``My chair is marked front and back with my name, and I need it shortly to go to physical therapy.'' Mother listens again, now with her eyes closed.

``Then let me help. I can call the supervisor's office to see if someone there is free to wheel back my . . . '' Mother breaks off and says with sincerity ``Many thanks, Mrs. Winters,'' and hangs up.

Almost immediately the chair appears. ``Shall I take you to physical therapy?'' the attendant asks.

``No, my daughter Elaine is visiting from . . . '' she pauses her concentration gone.

``Miami,'' I suggest.

`` . . . and she'll take me.''

My mother's eyes stay closed. Her white hair is thin. Her arms and cheeks look like she is melting imperceptibly like snow in the shade as winter wanes.

``Mom, do you want me to take you over to . . . ?''

Eyes still closed, she murmurs, ``Just said that sweetheart. I don't have therapy till Tuesday.''

Sunday morning at The Waverly ticks by. Only Holly's cards are on the windowsill. Old ones in the hall who cry out like macaws shriek and make my heart bounce. ``Nononono!'' shouts a woman pushing past with a walker, arguing with no one visible.

``Mom,'' I finally say, ``you rest, and I'll come back.''

``Yes, go home to the children,'' she whispers.

Now I feel bad. It's hardly enough of a visit, but she's tired. Dave is waiting in the lobby, wanting to complete the other 80 miles of this round trip.

I kiss her forehead, so much like kissing the foreheads of my sons when they were little. Lightly I hold her hand and look at her nails once more.

``Shall I? . . .'' She folds her hands.

``Give my love to Bernard and the girls,'' my mother says and falls asleep.

I go into the hall. Bernard is Holly's husband. Holly has girls. My mother may not even remember I came.

``How is your mother?'' Dave asks as soon as I walk into the lobby.

``Really good,'' I lie. ``She sends you her love.''

He's ready to go, but I borrow a pen from the always-unoccupied reception desk. In the guest book I write our names, a little larger than the space, a little darker than necessary. I add the date and notice the last three entries are also mine.

``You never visit her,'' my sister once accused me on the phone.

``Really, I do,'' I said, my voice going tremulous and unconvincing.

``Well, don't bother,'' she said. ``Mother doesn't notice.''

On the Turnpike I wonder what my mother does notice. After I visit The Waverly, I wonder about everything. The pay back on motherhood. The disarray of families. Whether people go to hell for putting their parents in places like that.
