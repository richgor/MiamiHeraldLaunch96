Published Thursday, May 9, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Panel to probe Bosnia-Iran arms deal

WASHINGTON -- (AP) -- Overriding Democratic objections of election-year politics, House Republicans pressed ahead Wednesday with plans to investigate the Clinton administration's role in allowing Iran to ship arms to the Bosnian government.

By a largely party-line vote of 224-187, the House created an eight-member select subcommittee to carry out the probe.

The subcommittee of the House International Relations Committee will be made up of five Republicans and three Democrats and have a $995,000 budget to carry out its six-month investigation.

Democrats pointed out that the select subcommittee is scheduled to complete its work a week before Election Day.

``This select committee is not only completely unnecessary but also completely political,'' said Rep. Martin Frost, D-Texas. He said the probe would provide ``one million dollars of work for the Dole presidential campaign.''

The Democrats argued that any probe ought to be carried out by existing committees. Creating a select subcommittee gives the panel its own budget to carry out the investigation.

Rep. Lincoln Diaz-Balart, a Miami Republican, said Congress has a responsibility to investigate the administration ``secretly giving a green light to the arming of the Bosnians by one of the most horrendous enemies of the American people.''

The White House has said it will cooperate with an investigation of the decision to allow Iranian arms shipments to go to Bosnia through Croatia in 1994 when a U.N. embargo against arms shipments was in effect. Administration officials have said that the Croatian government was told the United States would have no objection to the shipments.

Speaking on condition of anonymity, officials have said that President Clinton personally approved sending that message to Croatia.

Rep. Lee Hamilton of Indiana, the senior Democrat on the International Relations Committee, argued that there was no need to create a select subcommittee to carry out the investigation.

``Many members knew about the arms shipments at the time and they did not protest,'' said Hamilton, who pointed out that three other House committees -- including the full International Relations Committee -- are investigating the incident as well.
