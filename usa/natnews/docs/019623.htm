Published Friday, May 10, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

For the Friday Clipper

POLICE AGENCIES THAT HAVE LONG BEEN WARY OF NEWFANGLED DEVICES HAVE BEGUN TO EXPERIMENT WITH:
  • ``Live-scan'' systems that allow police to take an inkless fingerprint -- they roll the suspect's digit across a glass plate and instantly check it against a computer database. Eventually, police expect to have live-scan equipment in patrol cars to provide immediate, positive identification of suspects on the street.

  • Patrol cars that can fire tagging devices at fleeing cars. Inside the projectiles are small radio transmitters that beam the car's location back to the pursuing officers, who can follow from a safe distance. The system gives officers an option besides engaging in a high-speed chase, which could endanger bystanders, or watching the bad guys drive off.

  • ``Smart guns'' that can be fired only by the owners. Sensors in the handgrip ``recognize'' the officer, allowing him or her to shoot, but no one else. Scientists think smart guns could reduce the number of officers killed each year; one in seven of those slain are shot with their own weapons.

  • Restraining foam that traps suspects in a thick wad of sticky glue. Police officials say that the foam is effective -- it originally was developed to immobilize potential intruders at nuclear-weapons facilities -- but that cleanup is slow and arduous.

  • Devices that send out electronic pulses that burn out the electrical systems of escaping cars, forcing them to stop.

  • Backseat airbags that officers can use to control unruly suspects.

  • Personal-monitoring devices that continuously notify headquarters of an officer's exact location and transmit a full-color video of the scene via a tiny camera attached to the officer's shirt.
