`WE CAN MEND OUR SOCIAL FABRIC': President Clinton.

Clinton makes pitch for public service

STATE COLLEGE, Pa. -- (AP) -- Seizing an issue that was once a Republican domain, President Clinton said Friday that ``we desperately need more citizen involvement'' to remedy America's problems with crime, teen pregnancy and welfare.

In a commencement address to Pennsylvania State University's graduate school, Clinton said there's been such a buildup of violence and gangs and drugs that ``we've almost become numb to it. A lot of us may even be resigned to it.''

But he said Americans should ``refuse to accept this as a normal and unavoidable and irreversible condition. I believe we can mend our social fabric.''

He urged every middle school and high school to make public service part of its curriculum and said colleges should use federal funds to orient work-study toward community service. He said the government would match community grants -- up to $500 -- to reward high school students who do significant work for their communities.

``We have to find ways to build strong communities and we have got to find ways to get more personal responsibility from all our citizens,'' Clinton said.

The speech was the first in a series of at least three commencement addresses billed by the White House as presenting Clinton's vision and agenda for a second term. The president will speak on America's global leadership at the Coast Guard Academy in New London, Conn., on May 22, and on economic issues June 4 at Princeton University in New Jersey.

Under Clinton's plan for high school students, the government would match each dollar raised by communities, schools, businesses and civic groups up to $500. The money would come from the $150 million increase requested for the president's national service program.
