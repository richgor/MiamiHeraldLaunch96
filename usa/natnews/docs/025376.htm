Published Thursday, May 9, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

New drug avoids common side effect

Anti-depressant less likely to cause sexual problems

Newsday

NEW YORK -- The latest drugs to help fight depression may also hurt sexual function, prompting many to abandon the treatments. Now, a study has pinpointed a drug that allows people to treat the devastating disorder and keep their intimate relations healthy.

Utah researchers have found that one of the latest anti-depressants on the market -- Serzone, manufactured by Bristol-Myers Squibb -- was much less likely to leave patients with miserable sex lives.

Doctors first noticed that as many as 80 percent of patients receiving the newest antidepressants, including Prozac and Zoloft, were experiencing some form of sexual dysfunction, not as a remnant of the depression but as a side effect of the treatment.

Noting this effect, Dr. James Ferguson of the University of Utah recruited a number of people who experienced sexual problems while on antidepressants for a study of the issue. Forty-four patients were then put on either Zoloft, a medication sold by Pfizer, or on Serzone.

They were followed for eight weeks and continuously answered a series of intimate questions about their sex lives. In the Zoloft-treated group, 80 percent re-experienced sexual dysfunction compared with 30 percent of those taking Serzone.

``Patients generally don't want to talk about these problems,'' said Ferguson, who said that the sexual difficulties run from delayed ejaculation to loss of desire.

Ferguson and others say that Serzone does not work like Zoloft or Prozac but alleviates depression in similar ways.

Ferguson reported his findings Wednesday at the American Psychiatric Association's annual meeting in New York.

Other studies presented at the conference support this finding. No one knows why or how sexual function is targeted by these drugs.

But depressed patients should think twice before switching drugs, doctors say. There is strong evidence from a Harvard study that taking a break -- such as a weekend free of medication -- will prevent the bothersome side effects without compromising the treatment.

Harvard's Dr. Anthony Rothschild found that these drug holidays worked for the antidepressants Paxil and Zoloft but not Prozac. Also a common antihistamine has been used that reverses this side effect.

``These are powerful, well-tolerated drugs and the importance is that they work in alleviating serious depression,'' said Pfizer's Dr. Wilma Harrison. ``Let's focus on what's important here.''
