Published Tuesday, May 7, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

JACK WESTON

Prolific comic actor

New York Times Service

NEW YORK -- Jack Weston, the chubby, jowly actor who elevated anguish to a comic art in a 40-year career on television, in the movies and on Broadway, has died. He was 71.

Weston died Friday at Lenox Hill Hospital in New York City. He had lymphoma for
six years, his wife said.

Weston appeared in hundreds of television productions, two dozen movies and another two dozen Broadway plays.

Woody Allen hired Weston for his acclaimed role as a sleazy personal manager in The Floating Lightbulb, which led to a Tony nomination for Weston in 1981.

One of his more acclaimed roles was in The Four Seasons, the 1981 movie with Alan Alda, Carol Burnett and Rita Moreno that led to a spinoff television series starring Weston in 1984.

His Hollywood years began on a whim in the spring of 1958 when he and his first wife, Marjorie Redmond, abruptly quit their parts in the Broadway hit Bells Are Ringing and struck out for Los Angeles in a vintage Volkswagen, expecting to return to New York in the fall.

But it turned out to be an 18-year stay as Weston appeared almost constantly on television. And his wife did even better: a 10-year stint as the Cool Whip lady.

In much of his early work, Weston was the villain, most memorably as a man who terrorizes Audrey Hepburn in the 1967 cult classic Wait Until Dark.
