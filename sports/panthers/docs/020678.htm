Published Thursday, May 9, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Panthers go for trial separation

MacLean breaks up front line

By DAVID J. NEAL
Herald Sports Writer

The Panthers' top two scorers of the 1995-96 season have been married as linemates most of the campaign. With each going through tough times in the playoffs, they and the coaching staff
agreed a trial separation might help.

Going into tonight's Game 4 of the Eastern Conference semifinal with Philadelphia, right wing Scott Mellanby and third-year center Rob Niedermayer have a total of three playoff points. And three is not a magic number after 14 playoff games.

``It's fortunate that a lot of other guys have stepped up, but for us to win this series, we have to contribute,'' Mellanby said.

As a line, Mellanby, Niedermayer and Johan Garpenlov have five playoff points, the same as defensive defenseman Paul Laus. So, they were split over the past two days. Garpenlov was scratched for Game 3 and doesn't know if he'll be back in Game 4.

``I didn't like the way he played the last two games,'' Panthers Coach Doug MacLean replied when asked about Garpenlov's benching.

Then, in practice Wednesday, with left wing Bill Lindsay sitting out with a swollen eye, Garpenlov skated with Mellanby and Tom Fitzgerald. Niedermayer was with Radek Dvorak, who played with he and Mellanby Tuesday, and Martin Straka.

``I'm part of the Czech Republic,'' Niedermayer laughed.

Mellanby, too, has retained his sense of humor, chuckling, ``It would be nice to get some kind of bounce. I'd take one like Mario got last night. I'd actually take one off my face.''

Pittsburgh's Lemieux figured out yet another way to score Tuesday night against the Rangers when a shot went off his noggin, off Ulf Samuelsson's face . . . nothing but net. Lemieux got credit for the goal as the last Penguin to touch the puck.

Actually, that goal demonstrates something neither Niedermayer nor Mellanby has been doing in the postseason: when in doubt, fire at the net, the way Laus did to get Florida's lone goal Tuesday night. Combined, Niedermayer and Mellanby are averaging two shots a game.

Early in Game 3, Mellanby had two defenders occupied when Niedermayer picked up the puck in the right face-off circle, a usual shooting spot for him. Instead he tried to pass back to Mellanby, who was being hauled down for a penalty on Philadelphia. A Niedermayer shot would have yielded as much and at least the possibility of a goal.

``Sometimes, there's a little bit of confidence lacking when you haven't scored in a while so you tend to do that,'' MacLean said.

After Mellanby cracked that he always shoots because he doesn't have the skills to do otherwise, he said of Niedermayer, ``He's got a great shot, and we want to use it.''

``I really have to have more of a shoot-first mentality,'' said Niedermayer, who hasn't experienced a slump like this since his first year playing juniors in Medicine Hat. ``You look at the goals scored in the playoffs, you just have to get the puck at the net.''

Perhaps that will produce the break that both Mellanby and Niedermayer feel they need.

``It's frustrating because I didn't feel we were that far away,'' Mellanby said.

``The last three games have been a lot better,'' Niedermayer said. ``I'm starting to skate better, do the little things . . . it's starting to come. I can feel it starting to come.''

Tuesday, they were joined by Dvorak, who missed the net when Rhett Warrener sent him in one-on-one with Philadelphia goalie Ron Hextall, saw a drive from the slot stopped and saw a two-on-one with Niedermayer broken up.

``He's a guy who could use a break,'' Mellanby said.

``Dvorak had a couple of great chances, but the tough part is he's 18 years old in alone on a veteran goalie,'' MacLean said.

  • Flyers fans, Philadelphia media and ESPN's Barry Melrose chorused that Panthers followers should quit whining about Eric Lindros' stickwork during Game 2. Either accept it as ``playoff hockey'' or get Lindros back when the game doesn't matter.

    Yet, when Panthers defenseman Ed Jovanovski took a swing at Lindros as time ran out Tuesday, it was considered an undisciplined act in the Philadelphia media.

    Jovanovski said it wasn't premeditated, but he shouldn't have to tolerate Lindros' extraneous run after slapping the puck the length of the ice to run out the clock.

    ``I could've raised the puck and tried to hurt someone, but I kept it on the ice,'' Jovanovski said. ``It was the heat of the moment. Whatever happens -- a misconduct or whatever -- isn't going to change the game.''

  • Flyers goalie Ron Hextall, a man with a sullied reputation himself, said of the hullabaloo over Lindros, ``Nobody wants to be called a cheap player and anybody who says that [about Lindros] is extremely uneducated.''
