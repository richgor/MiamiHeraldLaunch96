Published Tuesday, May 7, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Getting goal(ie) oriented

Hextall: Focus on teams, not Vanbiesbrouck vs. me

By GARY MILES
The Philadelphia Inquirer

Ron Hextall doesn't buy all this head-to-head matchup business. The Flyers goaltender understands why he and Florida Panthers goalie John Vanbiesbrouck are poster boys for this Eastern Conference semifinal series.

Oh, he knows that both goalies are playing
their brains out.

He just doesn't buy the spotlight.

``It's the Flyers playing the Florida Panthers. It's not me playing Vanbiesbrouck,'' Hextall said Monday as the Flyers prepared for tonight's Game 3 of the best-of-seven series at Miami Arena.

Hextall and Vanbiesbrouck -- make that the Flyers and the Panthers -- are tied at one victory apiece.

``People try to make it Eric Lindros vs. Mark Messier when we play the Rangers, and it's not like that, either,'' Hextall said. ``It was me against Tampa Bay's Daren Puppa in the first round. It's always goalie against goalie. It's the nature of the position.''

But it's not always this goalie against that goalie. Hextall and Vanbiesbrouck are two of the NHL's most-experienced and successful goalies. Both have won Vezina Trophies. Both were coveted by Bob Clarke, the Flyers' general manager, who served as the Florida GM in 1993.

And both have been dominating in the playoffs. Hextall is 5-3 with a 2.08 goals-against average and a .911 save percentage. Vanbiesbrouck is 5-2 with a 2.55 goals-against average and a .930 save percentage.

Both have allowed just 17 goals.

``Both goalies have been very good in making big stops at key times,'' Flyers Coach Terry Murray said. ``Hexy made some big saves at the end of Game 2 when we were holding a 3-2 lead, and Vanbiesbrouck made some big stops in Game 1.

``Goaltenders are the key people in the series. They always will be.''

Hextall has been a key player all season for the Flyers. He won 31 games, the second most of his career, and had a career-low 2.17 goals-against average. He had four shutouts and played more than 3,000 minutes.

And he did it all having to adjust to the Flyers' stifling defense. Never before had Hextall seen so few shots in a season, he said.

``It's an adjustment playing in front of such a defensive team,'' Hextall said. ``You don't handle the puck as much, and the pace of the game is different. I've definitely had to change how I play and think a little bit.''

Indeed, the Flyers outshot the Panthers, 65-41, in the first two games, and Florida managed to get off more than eight shots in a period only once. Since the Flyers blasted at least 14 shots at Vanbiesbrouck in each of the three periods of Saturday's 3-2 victory in Game 2, the Florida goalie would appear to be under more pressure.

Maybe not, Hextall said.

``Players put pressure on themselves,'' he said. ``Our teammates don't pressure us to play differently or better. We know it's a team game. Look at Game 2. Vanbiesbrouck played a great game and lost.''
