Published Sunday, April 28, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Boomers are big on bed, bath

HIGH POINT, N.C. -- Bless those baby boomers. They appreciate quality and are willing to pay for it.

So says Jerry Epperson, the furniture industry guru who gives a ``state of the economy'' talk to manufacturers at the International Home Furnishings Market every April.

``The baby boomers [77 million Americans aged 32 to 50] have had all the kids they are going to have, but [the children] are still living at home,'' said Epperson of Mann, Armistead & Epperson, Richmond, Va. ``This means they are buying bigger houses and they are buying more furniture and better furniture.''

Bigger houses doesn't mean manufacturers should produce larger furniture, however.

``Bigger houses mean more bedrooms,'' said Epperson. ``We're selling more beds and bedding.''

However, one room that is growing is the bathroom.

``TV and exercise equipment are becoming standard for the master bath,'' Epperson said. ``Next thing you know we'll be entertaining in the bath.''
* * *

  • Updated Retro look. One of designer Larry Laslo's new sofas for Directional's High Society collection is named South Beach -- for you-know-where. It is sleekly sculptural with a curving back that glides into low arms. Tightly upholstered in silk on all sides, it can float in the middle of a room. The sofa will retail for $2,399 and the matching ottoman, $729.
    * * *

  • Ottoman empire. There are lots of reasons to put your feet up these days. Ottomans, many of them with storage compartments, can be found in most furniture collections.

    This handy piece of furniture also is growing, matching chairs and even sofas in width. The really big ottomans -- called ``table ottomans'' because they double as cocktail tables -- are five feet long in some cases. Just the place for an unexpected overnight guest.
    * * *

  • Neat ideas. Furniture designers may be credited for helping the slovenly get their act together. The designers are including velvet or felt-lined jewelry compartments in top drawers of bureaus and silver organizers in dining room pieces.

    Many new desks and bureaus have hidden compartments to stash jewelry or cash.
    * * *

  • Novelty prints. La-Z-Boy introduced a recliner upholstered in rock 'n' roll-themed fabric in red, white and blue.

    Thayer Coggin showed a berry patch pattern on a chair that looks like needlepoint from afar.

    Drexel-Heritage displayed a topiary print on the skirt of an ottoman.

    Several upholstery makers chose wild animal prints for occasional pieces such as small chairs.
    * * *

  • Less is not more. Sofas and chairs aren't wearing as much fringe or braid as they did a couple of seasons ago, but fancy is still ``in.'' Marge Carson, a manufacturer of contemporary furniture that's often oversized, introduced a sofa with 19 yards of braid outlining the frame.

    Designers of leather seating have always used nail heads to secure the material. Now the nails -- as big as silver dollars in some cases -- are part of the trim.

    ``Important'' buttons are also being used as trim, sometimes boldly marching down the back or along the skirt of a sofa.
