Published Sunday, April 28, 1996, in the Miami Herald.

Expo shows wares with French accent

By LIZ SEYMOUR New York Times Service

HIGH POINT, N.C. -- On Day 3 of the International Home Furnishings Market, the world's largest retail furniture expo, buyers hurrying between showroom buildings paused while the skies darkened and the temperature dropped. A funnel cloud that someone reported at the city limits never did touch down, but weather remained on everyone's mind.

Retailers from the Northeast and Midwest arrived at the semiannual market after a disastrous winter of record snowfalls. Continued consumer caution and the rumble of higher interest rates did not improve their mood.

Shipments of new furniture in the $48.7 billion industry were down 2 percent in the first two months of this year, the American Furniture Manufacturers Association said.

This spring, it was a ``Waiting to Exhale'' market without clearly developed trends. The 2,300 showrooms generally displayed a kind of end-of-millennium nostalgia. A shift toward yellow-green and dark finishes evoked the 1940s, the '50s, the '60s and even the '70s.

Mohair, tweed and chrome -- the last used lavishly by Ralph Lauren in a $15,885 four-poster bed and an $8,265 trestle table for Henredon -- threatened to return.

Even ``Mediterranean,'' a word all but banned from the design lexicon for two decades, was back, most notably in Thomasville's 115-piece Renaissance Collection of ornate, large-scale furniture.

Goods ordered at the April market will not appear in stores until the fall.

Along with the color shift came a definite tilt toward Europe, away from Shaker, Mission and other American designs of the last few years. France was the country of choice.

``This is European styling for the American market,'' said Tom Tilley, senior vice president of Century Furniture, which uses a robot to distress and add gouge marks and wormholes to the maple wood in its new Town and Country collection.

Even contemporary style was spoken with a French accent. The Art Deco designer Jean-Michel Frank, who died in 1941, was the most famous trend-setter not in attendance. His angular work inspired the carriage-trade maker John Widdicomb as well as Mitchell Gold, whose customers include Pottery Barn.

The droopy slipcovered look (Early Windstorm) was clearly on its way out. The freshest upholstered chairs and sofas looked tailored and taut, as though someone had put a skate key in the back of each and given it a few turns.

Out, too, was rumpled washed linen and faded chintz: New fabrics often mixed cotton with a little rayon or polyester. Just enough for sheen; not enough to raise your barometric pressure.
