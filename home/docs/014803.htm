Unusual griddle has grease catcher

By ANITA GOLD
Knight-Ridder News Service

Q.

I collect cast iron and have a piece I can't find in any books. I'm enclosing a description and photo of it and hope you can identify it. I got it from an 89-year-old woman who said her mother cooked on it in a fireplace.

The piece, which is old and made of cast iron (and which presumably is a bacon griddle once used for fireplace cooking) has a heavy rectangular-shaped pan measuring 13 1/2 inches long, 10 1/2 inches wide and one-half inch deep, which stands on four three-inch-high tapered legs.

It has an 8 1/2-inch-long handle that's an inch thick and is flat on top and rounded on the bottom, marked with a raised X on top of the handle where it's attached to the back of the pan. The pan has a 1-inch-deep and 10 1/2-inch-wide grease drain across the end. How can I find out more about this 16-pound piece?

A.

Your presumption is right. The piece is indeed a griddle complete with a grease trough for collecting grease or frying fat. It dates from the late 18th or early 19th Century and is most desirable and uncommon, being cast instead of wrought -- as most examples of this type are -- and having an X (or witchmark) on its handle, put there by the maker to ward off witches and demons.

To check out griddles with a solid frying or cooking surface (rather than an open gratelike one) or any other early fireplace cooking implements, write to Pat Guthman, Pat Guthman Antiques Inc., 281 Pequot Ave., Southport, Conn. 06490. Enclose a photo of the piece (or pieces) and a self-addressed, stamped envelope for a reply.

Also available from Pat is her show-and-tell video, Hot and Heavy -- 18th Century Iron for Cooking at the Fireplace, which costs $29.95 plus $4 shipping.

Q.

How can I find the value of an original 1930s Betty Boop cartoon cel? Is there a publication in which I can offer it for sale?

A.

Write to the Betty Boop Fan Club, founder Bobbie West, 6025 Fullerton Ave., No. 2, Buena Park, Calif. 90621. Enclose a self-addressed, stamped envelope for a reply. Send $10 for an annual membership, which includes a quarterly newsletter.

Q.

I have a picture of Tom Thumb dated 1863. How can I find its value, and where can I sell it?

A.

Write to Hy Roth, an expert on little people, who wrote a definitive and historical book on the subject, now out of print. He buys and appraises historical photographs and tintypes, as well as old and odd cameras. His address is 1300 Ashland, Evanston, Ill. 60201. Enclose a self-addressed, stamped envelope for a reply, appraisal or offer.

Q.

I am interested in finding a collector who will pay the right price for a large collection of original 78 rpm jazz and swing recordings from the '30s and '40s in pristine condition. I also have many 33 1/3 rpm records of the same type. Can you help?

A.

Write to the International Association of Jazz Record Collectors, Phil Pospychala, 15745 W. Birchwood Lane, Libertyville, Ill. 60048-5101. Enclose a description of the records, stating their condition, and enclose a self-addressed, stamped envelope.

Q.

Who would be interested in buying a 1948 copy of The Architectural Forum magazine featuring Frank Lloyd Wright and his drawings?

A.

To sell anything Frank Lloyd Wright-connected, especially furniture and metalware, etc., write to the John Toomey Gallery, 818 North Blvd., Oak Park, Ill. 60301.

Write to Anita Gold, P.O. Box 597401, Chicago, Ill. 60659. Enclose a self-addressed, stamped envelope. Not all letters can be answered. Priority will be given to those that include a copy of the column.
